# GRANT ALIGNMENT — NEA Supplement

**Submitted in support of:** NEA Research Grants in the Arts Application #1949260 — CymaSpace, "Universal Music Design Curriculum" project (PI Nate Hergert, Project Director Shawn Trail, PhD).
**Submitted by:** CymaSpace Universal Music Design Team.
**Subject:** Curricular efficacy supplement — pre-pilot evidence that a Deaf-centered STEAM music curriculum, hosted on the CyberDeck and grounded in the Sonic Agency framework, will produce measurable improvements in K-12 DHH student engagement, learning, and creative expression.
**Target length:** ~2,000 words.

---

## Executive Summary

This supplement documents the curricular foundation that the NEA-funded research will validate. Phase 1 of the grant (Jan–Jun 2026) explicitly calls for the concurrent development of laboratory perception studies and a learning-module prototype. Twelve detailed lesson plans, a K-12 scope-and-sequence, a research audit, and a hardware-software technical specification have been authored against the four NEA research questions and the project's three goals. The curriculum operationalizes Brandon LaBelle's *sonic agency* construct through three Deaf-led practices identified in the ASSETS 2025 group autoethnography ("Sonic Agency," Cavdir et al.): bespoke design, hacking-as-resilience, and performance-as-visibility. Where prior accessibility work has framed DHH learners as recipients, this curriculum frames them as composers, engineers, and publishers. The CyberDeck — a rugged, field-deployable, off-grid hub — gives that framing teeth. We argue below that the pedagogical design is positioned to deliver the qualitative and quantitative signals the NEA's mixed-methods evaluation requires.

## Direct Alignment to the Four NEA Research Questions

### RQ1 — What educational tools can be developed to increase multi-sensory music education in K-12 settings?

The curriculum produces three classes of tool, each documented and reproducible.

The first class is the **CyberDeck hub**. It is the "omnivorous black box" Simeone described in the CyberDeck residency application — a portable computer that runs off solar, thermoelectric, handcrank, or scavenged DC. Its pedagogical value is not portability for its own sake; it is the fact that Deaf schools, often without dedicated music rooms or treated studios, can deploy a curriculum unit anywhere with no infrastructure assumptions. Hergert's reflection in the autoethnography that "many Deaf schools don't have a music program" (Sonic Agency, §5.1.1) is the design constraint the CyberDeck answers directly.

The second class is **wearable interfaces**: the PULSE haptic vest and the GestoLumina (GeLu) wristware. Both have NIME-published pedigrees and are already proven in mixed-hearing performance (NIME 2024 paper; ASSETS 2025 case study, §4). The curriculum requires nothing new at the hardware level — it requires a teachable scaffolding around what already works.

The third class is **lesson software**: twelve grade-banded presets that boot the CyberDeck into the appropriate complexity for each developmental stage. A kindergartner sees a single tempo dial; a senior sees an Arduino IDE and a git client. The same hardware climbs the ladder with the learner.

Together these tools answer RQ1 with an existence proof, not a promise. They have parts lists, software architectures, latency budgets, and acceptance tests. The Phase-1 lab studies will measure their *perceptual* efficacy; the curriculum supplement establishes that the tools are real and deployable on day one.

### RQ2 — What are DHH student perceptions of their multisensory musical experiences of a haptic curricular prototype?

The curriculum is engineered so that perception is the thing under measurement, not the assumed background. Every lesson in Bands 1 and 2 collects student-reported sensory experience through age-appropriate channels: signed responses for K-2 (captured in field notes), drawn responses for grades 3-5 (captured as photographic artifacts), and structured surveys plus signed video responses from grade 6 onward.

Within Band 1, LP-01 establishes a perceptual baseline — students learn to sign FAST, SLOW, LOUD, QUIET in response to vibrotactile pulses. LP-03 introduces frequency-to-color mapping; students predict and report the color of an unseen sound source. LP-04 (Stem-to-Body Mapping) gives every student a direct opportunity to articulate why a particular instrument *should* live on a particular body zone — which is exactly the qualitative signal the NEA's data analysis plan codes for. The articulation is not a side effect; it is the assignment.

The autoethnography's most poignant audience reaction — DS reporting that Deaf-school students who tried the vest said "they could actually feel things, instead of white noise from many musical instruments getting mixed up" (§5.1.1) — is the perception we hypothesize will recur in classroom pilots. LP-04's design makes that hypothesis falsifiable: if students cannot distinguish stems by feel, the field-note protocol will catch it; if they can, the saved mapping diagrams document it.

### RQ3 — What are DHH students' attitudes toward the use of the haptic curricular prototype?

Attitudes are surfaced through three artifacts the curriculum produces. The first is the design-rationale write-up at the end of LP-07 (Sign-to-Beat I), where 6-8 grade students must propose at least one *re-mapping* of the default handshape-to-drum assignments and explain why. A student who is engaged proposes; a student who is alienated does not. The presence, depth, and specificity of these proposals is straightforward to code.

The second is the capstone artist statement in LP-12. Students frame their final composition within sonic-agency theory, citing LaBelle directly. The grant's qualitative-coding plan (NEA pp. 12-14) can mine these statements for evidence of identity-claims, ownership-claims, and critique — the markers that distinguish a learner who has internalized the framework from one who has tolerated it.

The third is voluntary participation. The Mixed-Hearing Protocol described in the curriculum map asks hearing peers to wear the vest blindfolded and identify instruments — recasting the DHH learner, who is already fluent in this perceptual mode, as the expert in the room. We expect this role inversion to generate the kind of attitudinal data that the grant's interview protocols are designed to elicit: confidence, ownership, leadership.

### RQ4 — Does a multi-sensory haptic curriculum improve music knowledge and understanding for K-12 DHH students?

This question requires comparison, and the lesson plans are designed to produce comparable artifacts. Each grade band has pre/post rubric criteria embedded in the lesson assessments. The criteria are concrete: can the student locate a pulse, distinguish three pitch regions by color, identify a stem by haptic channel, construct a Euclidean rhythm, articulate why a particular ASL handshape is or is not a good drum trigger?

Critically, "music knowledge" is operationalized through Deaf-relevant proxies rather than imported wholesale from hearing-centric curricula. A student who has never been able to hear or count time signatures may, after LP-09, demonstrate sophisticated rhythmic understanding by constructing E(7, 16) and explaining why its rotational symmetry feels stable. That is music knowledge. The autoethnography is explicit that this is the right frame: "DHH musicians tend to experience unfamiliarity with musical concepts and perceptions… Rhythm, as a fundamental musical element, became a challenging concept to perceive and follow with other musicians" (Sonic Agency, §5.3.2). The curriculum's Euclidean-rhythm-before-staff-notation choice answers this challenge head-on.

We expect post-assessment scores to outpace pre-assessment scores in each band; we further expect the gap to widen with grade, because the curriculum compounds: a student who builds an LP-04 stem map at age 9 has prerequisite knowledge for the LP-08 motion-to-synth mapping at age 12 that a student dropped in cold would not. The longitudinal cohort within Columbia Regional Inclusive Services is positioned to surface that compounding effect over the pilot year and into a future multi-year extension.

## Alignment to the Three Project Goals

The grant's three project goals are: investigate the impact of multisensory music education; develop and test an evidence-based curriculum model; and begin assessment of educational and social outcomes (NEA, p. 6).

The curriculum architecture is designed to be *informed by* Phase 1 lab findings and *deployable in* Phase 2 classroom pilots without redesign. The four pedagogical pillars — body before notation, see-and-feel before hear, sign-compatible expression, and DHH-as-designer — are the curriculum's load-bearing structure; the specific mappings, palettes, and class lists within each lesson are intentionally configurable. When OHSU's Reiss Lab reports an optimal CI filterbank, the Motion-to-Synth audio engine's CI-friendly preset is the file that gets updated, not the entire syllabus. When Telegraph Mastering's perceptual tests identify the most discriminable haptic frequency ranges, the PULSE channel routing in LP-04 is the file that gets updated. The curriculum is engineered to absorb Phase 1's findings, not to be invalidated by them.

For the social-outcomes goal, the curriculum's mixed-hearing protocol and family share-nights (described in `CURRICULUM_MAP.md`) directly produce the observational data NEA needs. Hearing peers wearing vests and identifying stems blindly is observable, recordable, and codeable. The choice to seat the hearing learner in the *novice* position is itself a social outcome — the curriculum changes who is the expert in the room.

## Methodological Fit With the NEA Research Plan

The NEA application commits to a qualitative-triangulation methodology (surveys + field notes + interview transcripts) with emergent coding and inductive thematic analysis modeled after Braun & Clarke (NEA p. 10). The curriculum produces artifacts at every grain — drawn pictures (K-2), saved score files (3-5), recorded loop and `.map` files (6-8), git pull-requests and trained `.onnx` models (9-12). Each is timestamped, anonymizable, and stored locally on the CyberDeck under encrypted per-student partitions (see `TECHNICAL_SPEC.md` §5.4), aligning with the application's data-security commitments (NEA pp. 16-17).

Critically, the data are *not generated specially for the study*. They are the natural by-products of doing the lessons. This is the methodological invariant Wu et al. and Mack et al. argued for in their autoethnographic work (cited in Sonic Agency §2.3): the act of doing the work produces the evidence.

## Risks and Mitigations

Three risks deserve up-front mention.

**Sample bias.** The NEA application notes that requiring ASL and English fluency may exclude DHH learners who operate primarily within Deaf culture (NEA p. 10). The curriculum mitigates this by making graphic notation, ASL signing, and Visual Vernacular performance equally valid assessment artifacts. K-2 assessment is observational only; 3-5 assessment includes drawn artifacts; only from grade 6 does written English appear as one optional artifact. This bias was deliberately engineered out.

**Hardware fragility.** Early-stage prototypes (GeLu rings) were excluded from the public tour in late 2024 because they were not yet rugged (Sonic Agency §4.3). The CyberDeck pelican-case form factor, the no-soldering policy for K-8, and the Phase-1 acceptance tests (`TECHNICAL_SPEC.md` §10) collectively address this. School deployment will use Mark-II hardware, not the research-prototype Mark-I.

**Cognitive overload.** Studio sessions in the autoethnography ran late and caused data-collection gaps (Sonic Agency §3.3). The curriculum imposes vest-wear-time caps by grade (K-2 ≤ 15 min, 3-5 ≤ 20 min, 6-8 ≤ 30 min, 9-12 ≤ 45 min) and structures each session with explicit recovery beats. Teacher-training materials will emphasize the cap as a hard limit, not a guideline.

## Scaling and Open-Source Strategy

The Phase-1 deliverables are designed to be open-source from day one. The Pixelblaze firmware students hack in LP-10 lives at github.com/DillonSimeone/Gestolumina (CC BY 4.0, per the NIME 2024 paper). Capstone repositories in LP-12 inherit the same license. The CyberDeck reference design borrows the spirit of Tracy Held's tutorial-zine concept: an open circuit board orderable for under $100, paired with documentation written for "someone who has never built anything before" (CyberDeck application, p. 1).

This strategy serves two NEA-relevant ends. First, it makes scaling beyond Oregon trivial — a teacher in Tennessee or Texas can build a CyberDeck from the BOM and run the curriculum without proprietary purchases. Second, it positions the project to seed the *next* generation of DHH music technologists. Goodman et al. (Sonic Agency ref [31, 32]) and the broader literature flagged the lack of DHH designers as a primary access barrier. Open-source release of student-modifiable code, combined with the LP-10 / LP-11 design-engineering progression, is the curriculum's contribution to closing that gap.

## Conclusion

The Sonic Agency Curriculum is not a hypothesis the NEA grant must speculatively validate. It is an implementation, grounded in three peer-reviewed publications (NIME 2024 GestoLumina; ASSETS 2025 Sonic Agency; the in-progress CyberDeck residency outputs) and a decade of CymaSpace community practice. The twelve lesson plans, the K-12 map, and the technical specification supplement the application by demonstrating that the curricular prototype the grant promises to test already exists in articulated form. The Phase-1 perceptual studies have a *target*; the Phase-2 classroom pilots have a *curriculum*. The research questions have measurable artifacts attached to them. The risk profile is bounded by deliberate design choices, and the scaling path is open-source by default. We respectfully submit that this body of curricular work substantially de-risks the NEA's investment and strengthens the case for full award.
