The Labyrinth of the Green Orbs
I. The Discovery of the Heresy

The Nibelheim incident did not culminate in fire, but in an exhausting silence.

History, which is fond of repetitions and crude theatricality, records that the soldier Sephiroth, upon discovering the monstrous nature of his birth in the basement of the Shinra Manor, intended to burn the village to the ashes from which his "mother" was denied. This is an error born of a fundamental misunderstanding of the soldier's mind. Sephiroth did not possess the vulgar passion required for arson. He possessed the geometric clarity of an executioner or a librarian.

On the third night of his isolation among the decaying leather volumes of Gast’s library, Sephiroth did indeed learn that he was a textile woven from the threads of a cosmic cadaver. The shock was not emotional, but taxonomic. He realized he did not belong to the genus of men, nor to the genus of gods, but to an unnamed, intrusive category of things that should not exist.

To calm the sudden, vertical vertigo of this realization, he began to sort. He took from his pockets, and from the velvet-lined cases of the laboratory, the spheres of crystallized mako—the Materia.

He did not arrange them by color, which is the superficial method of the mercenary, but by their specific refractive indices under the light of a dying tallow candle. He placed a sphere of Fire beside a sphere of Ice, separated by a fragment of Restore, forming an irregular rhombus on the dusty mahogany floor.

It was a minor arrangement, an idle configuration meant to pass the hour before he decided whether to destroy the world or simply withdraw from it.

Then, he felt the draft.

There were no windows open in the basement. Yet, the air above the rhombus was moving. It was a cold, perfectly circular current that smelled faintly of salt and very old grass. Sephiroth lowered his hand.

The air was warmer near the floor, which was correct. But as his fingers neared the apex of the Ice sphere, the temperature rose sharply. He withdrew a small silver pocket-chronometer—a gift from the President—and held its mechanical balance-wheel over the center of the rhombus.

The hand of the watch, which usually ticked with the stubborn, linear predictability of all Swiss mechanisms, began to accelerate. It did not merely spin; it blurred, its tiny brass gears whining under an impossible, unprovoked momentum.

Sephiroth sat on his heels. The candles had long since sputtered out, but the room was no longer dark. The four spheres were glowing, not with the dull, phosphorescent hum of harvested Lifestream, but with a sharp, aggressive glare that cast long, geometric shadows against the brickwork.

He took his notebook. He calculated the mass of the Materia. He calculated the maximum caloric yield of a standard mako extraction plant. He subtracted the resistance of the air, the friction of the mahogany, the cosmic constant of the planet’s gravity.

The equation on the paper refused to balance. Every time he drew the line beneath the column, a small, stubborn integer remained on the right side. It was a positive value. It was small—perhaps three watts of power—but it was independent of any input.

The world, Sephiroth knew, was a closed ledger. One paid for light with oil; one paid for life with blood. This was the law of the Cetra, the law of Shinra, and the law of thermodynamics.

The rhombus disagreed. It was producing three watts of light and heat out of nothing but its own syntax.

Sephiroth forgot about Jenova. He forgot about the corporate entity that signed his paychecks. He forgot the name of the blonde infantryman who stood guard at the door. He sat down in the dust, crossed his legs, and began to add a fifth sphere.
II. The Report of the Subalterns

In June, the Department of Logistics officially changed Sephiroth’s status from Delayed to Unaccounted For. By August, the word MIA appeared in the ledgers, quickly replaced by the whispered, more terrifying epithet of Rogue.

A squad of third-class soldiers, led by a dull-witted sergeant named Weiss, was sent to Nibelheim to reclaim the properties of the company—namely, the Masamune sword and the silver pauldrons.

They found the manor untouched. The villagers spoke of no fire, no madness, only a silence that had settled over the mountain like a heavy wool coat. When the soldiers breached the laboratory door, they did not find a rebel or a god.

They found a garden of glass.

The basement had been cleared of its books. The shelves had been broken down to provide raw wood for small, precise wedges. The floor was covered by an immense, concentric labyrinth of Materia—thousands of them, green, yellow, red, and blue—arranged in a spiral that seemed to mimic the shape of certain ancient galaxies or the whorls of a human thumb.

Sephiroth sat at the center of this maze. He had discarded his armor; he wore only his trousers and a linen shirt stained with ink and graphite. His long silver hair was tied back with a piece of copper wire. He looked less like the hero of Wutai and more like a monk who had spent forty years copying a single, heretical verse of the Torah.

The air in the room was deafening. It did not roar like an engine; it hummed with the high, terrifying pitch of a billion bees. The temperature was seventy-eight degrees Celsius, yet nothing was burning. The light was so intense that two of the infantrymen suffered immediate, permanent scotoma and had to be led away, weeping into their hands.

"Sir," Sergeant Weiss said, his voice cracking under the pressure of the atmosphere. "The President requires your return."

Sephiroth did not look up from a small, yellowed sphere of Mystify he was balancing between two fingers.

"Tell the President," Sephiroth whispered—and his voice, though soft, was carried perfectly through the room by the strange geometry of the air—"that the ledger is broken. The house is leaking from the inside."
III. The Arrival of the Architect

Professor Hojo arrived on the third day of September. He did not bring soldiers; he brought three trunks of brass instruments, a black umbrella, and his own profound, malicious curiosity.

He walked into the basement without a mask, his eyes watering instantly from the ozone and the terrible, silent glare of the spiral. He stopped at the edge of the first ring of Materia—a sequence of blue All crystals paired with green Earth ones.

"You have neglected your duties," Hojo said, his voice like dry paper sliding across a desk. "The specimen in the tank upstairs is dehydrating."

"Look at the third radius, Professor," Sephiroth said. He had not slept in ninety days, yet his eyes were clear, dilated to the edge of the irises.

Hojo adjusted his spectacles. He took a gold-plated galvanometer from his pocket and lowered its probe toward the gap between a Lightning sphere and a Choco/Mog summon crystal.

The needle of the instrument did not merely register a current; it snapped off its pivot, piercing the glass face of the gauge.

Hojo frowned. He retrieved a second meter, then a third. He spent four hours measuring the distance between the spheres with a bone caliper. He calculated the volume of the room. He took a sample of the air and tested it for vaporized mako. There was none. The crystals were dry. They were not consuming the planet’s blood; they were not drawing from the reactors in Midgar.

"This is an error in measurement," Hojo muttered, though his fingers were trembling so violently he could barely hold his pencil. "A systemic resonance. A fluke of the local magnetic field."

"I have moved the array three feet to the left," Sephiroth said softly. "The output rose by two percent. I have substituted three spheres of Poison for three spheres of Fire. The output remained constant, but the light shifted into the ultra-violet. It is not a fluke, Professor. It is an adjective."

"An adjective?" Hojo sneered.

"A description of space," Sephiroth said. He finally looked up, his green eyes reflecting the impossible, blinding geometry of the floor. "The Materia are not tools for magic, Hojo. They are nouns. When you place them in this specific order, they form a sentence that the universe cannot answer with a negative. They are a statement that creates its own subject."

Hojo sat down on an upturned packing crate. He stared at the center of the labyrinth, where a single, common sphere of Ice—the cheapest variety sold in the shops of Sector 7—was emitting a continuous, silent beam of white heat that was currently melting a hole through the stone foundation toward the core of the mountain.

The system was producing seventy-four kilowatts of pure, unblemished kinetic energy. Every second, it grew stronger by a fraction of a milliwatt. It required no maintenance. It required no fuel. It was an engine that ate its own exhaust and grew fat on the remainder.

"It violates the Second Law," Hojo whispered. His face had gone the color of lard. "If the Director of Weapons learns of this..."

"The Director of Weapons is a small man who thinks in terms of explosions," Sephiroth said, his voice flat with an immense, cosmic boredom. "An explosion is merely a temporary disagreement between two states of matter. This is not a disagreement. This is a permanent revision of the text."

The two men sat in the brilliant, freezing heat of the cellar. Above them, the alien thing in the glass tank remained forgotten, its ancient, leathery face pressed against the glass, staring down into the darkness where her "son" had found something far older, and far more indifferent, than herself.
