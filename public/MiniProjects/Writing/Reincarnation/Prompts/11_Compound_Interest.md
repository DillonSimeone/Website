# Prompt for Compound Interest

The party sat on the hillside and watched the horizon.

By morning, there were foundations.

By the end of the week, there were buildings, and the buildings were wrong in the way that things from outside the reference frame are wrong — not incorrect, just unfamiliar, their proportions and materials and the implicit logic of their arrangement saying something about purpose that the party could feel but not yet read.

They spent a lot of time walking around with their necks craned.

The pipeline appeared on the fourth day and Deren found it first.

A wide structure running from the lower district down to the coastline, into the water, with a rhythmic low-pulse that you felt in your feet before you heard it. Deren followed it to the facility at the water's edge — a building that smelled like the ocean but clean, like the ocean's best intentions — and went inside and stood in front of the first stage of the filtration system and tried to understand what they were looking at.

Marcus was there, or had been recently. The work had his quality — economical, recursive, nothing present that wasn't doing something.

Seawater came in. The system read it the way Marcus read everything — thoroughly, down to the constituent parts — and separated them with a patience that wasn't patience because patience implied waiting and this didn't wait, it just sorted, continuously, in parallel, pulling fresh water one direction and everything else into other streams, each stream processed further, each material emerging somewhere in the facility as itself, isolated, available.

Magnesium. Calcium. Sodium. Trace elements that the party had no names for.

Deren stood in front of the output streams for a long time.

Why did humans waste all of this time mining, they thought, with a clarity that felt like something restructuring inside them. When all of this was just. Everywhere. In the water. In the dirt. In the air.

The answer, Deren slowly understood, was that you had to know what you were looking for before you could find it, and you had to know it was there before you looked, and you had to have the tools to separate it once you found it, and building those three things in sequence was what the previous centuries had actually been for, whether they'd known it or not.

They went to find Senna and tell her what they'd seen.

Marcus found the anomaly on the second day.

He'd been aware of the low background irregularity in the local mana since the map, and up close it resolved into something more specific — a node, old, not artificial, not quite natural in the way naturally occurring mana formations were natural. Something in between. Something that had been here long enough to become part of the landscape without anyone building it intentionally.

He spent three days sitting next to it before he touched it.

When he touched it he sat back and thought for a long time.

Then he went and built his house at the bottom of the ocean, and thought some more, and decided the anomaly was the most interesting thing he'd found in this world so far, which was saying something.

He had not told the party about the house. He'd mentioned it once, practically, before they left — I'll be at the ocean floor, the city is yours to run, I have no interest in administering anything — and they had accepted this with the equanimity of people who had stopped being surprised by him and had not yet finished the process of deciding what to feel about that.

The house was at the base of the kelp forest, where the light came down filtered green and slow and the surface was a shimmering ceiling forty meters up. The kelp moved in the current like long slow thoughts. Small fish moved through it with the specific purposefulness of things that had never considered whether their existence had a point.

Marcus sat in the house and looked up through the water at the sunlight.

He thought about the anomaly. He thought about the two motes and what they'd grow into. He thought about Cael's face when he'd watched Marcus build a small structure in the air beside the chair, and what it would look like in ten years when Cael had words for what he was seeing.

He thought about the people in the orbs, time-frozen, waiting for someone to know how to help them. He thought about what kind of institution would produce the people who knew how to do that, and what it would take to build that institution, and how long.

He thought about the woman with two children pulling them into a doorway when the party walked through the city.

He thought about Seran's daughter sleeping through the entire fall of a kingdom's assumptions.

He put those thoughts in a row and looked at them.

Then he started working, which was what he always did when the thinking had gone as far as thinking could go and it was time for the next part.
