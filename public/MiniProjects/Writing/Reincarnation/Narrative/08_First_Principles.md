# First Principles

Senna taught the way people teach when they've spent decades doing it — economically, without warmth or coldness, just information moving from one place to another with the minimum necessary friction. She pulled mana into visible structure without being asked to, showing the forms rather than describing them, and Marcus watched with the same quality of attention he gave everything, which was total.

Deren watched too. With an expression that was becoming familiar.

"The base construct for tissue regeneration," Senna said, holding the structure in the air between them. It was precise work — decades of refinement visible in how little waste there was. "We call it the Fourfold Root. It predates my order by several centuries at least."

Marcus looked at it for a long time without speaking.

Then he said: "Who decided on four."

Senna paused. "It's called the Fourfold Root because—"

"Because it has four anchoring points. I can see that. I'm asking who decided four was correct."

A silence.

"It's always been four," Senna said. Carefully. The careful wasn't defensiveness — it was precision, he thought, a person making sure they were representing the thing accurately. "The texts that describe the original derivation don't survive. What we have is the form."

"So nobody knows."

"Nobody living."

Marcus reached out and turned the construct slightly, looking at the geometry from a different angle. "May I?"

Senna looked at him for a moment. "Yes."

He touched the structure.

He didn't change it — just read it, thoroughly, the way he'd read Grak's magic system in that first week, following calls back to definitions, finding the load-bearing parts. The construct was good work. Genuinely good. Whoever had originally derived it had understood something real about how mana interfaced with biological tissue, had found a set of relationships that worked and encoded them into replicable form.

They'd also, visibly, stopped asking questions at a certain point.

"Three would be enough," he said. "For most applications. The fourth anchor is load-bearing in about fifteen percent of cases — severe trauma, certain types of corruption damage. For routine work it's just mass." He looked at Senna. "What does that cost, in practice? Maintaining four instead of three."

"Approximately—" Senna was doing mental math. "A third more mana expenditure over a full working session."

"And your healers work in sessions of what length before exhaustion."

"Four hours, roughly. A trained healer, five."

"So with three anchors where appropriate, five hours becomes seven and a half, roughly." Marcus let go of the construct. "How many people in your chapter could extend their working day by three hours if they knew when four anchors were necessary and when three were sufficient."

Senna was very still.

"All of them," she said.

"Does your order work in areas where healing demand exceeds available healer hours."

The question didn't need an answer. He could see it in her face — that specific exhaustion that lives behind the eyes of people who have spent years doing necessary work with insufficient means and have simply learned to carry the arithmetic of it.

"Yes," she said anyway.

Marcus nodded. He picked up a book, set it back down. "I'm not telling you the construct is wrong. It's right. I'm telling you it's a first draft that nobody went back to revise." He looked at the structure still hanging in the air. "That's most of what I've found here. Very good first drafts."

Deren said, quietly: "Can you show me."

Marcus looked at them. "The three-anchor variant?"

"Everything," Deren said. "Start from the beginning. Show me what you see when you look at it."

He started talking.

He hadn't intended it to become a session — he'd intended to ask a question, gather data, understand the lineage of a specific pattern. But Deren asked the kind of questions that created more questions, and Senna had a fifty-year foundation that meant she could follow him further than he'd expected, and Aldric was sitting forward in his chair in a way he'd given up trying to look casual about, and at some point Cael had moved closer without appearing to decide to and was listening to a discussion about magical theory with the focused attention of someone who understood more of it than anyone in the room would have predicted.

Marcus kept stopping and asking Cael things. What the metal does when you heat it past a certain temperature. Whether he'd noticed patterns in which tools failed and why. What he understood about why different metals behaved differently.

Cael's answers were empirical and specific and built entirely from observation with no theoretical scaffold, which meant he'd been building the scaffold himself from scratch without knowing that's what he was doing, and several of the conclusions he'd reached were correct.

"You've been doing metallurgy," Marcus said.

"I've been making tools," Cael said.

"Yes. And in doing that you've been doing metallurgy. The practice came first, the word for the practice comes later — but what you've been doing has a name in my world and people devoted their lives to it and what you've figured out on your own puts you somewhere in the early middle of what they knew."

Cael stared at him.

"That's either very impressive," Marcus said, "or an indication of how much systematic knowledge this world is leaving undeveloped." He considered. "Both, probably."

Yara said, from her chair where she'd been watching all of this with her characteristic still assessment: "You're looking for people."

Marcus looked at her.

"That's what this is," she said. "All of it. The questions. You're not just gathering data about the world. You're finding out who in it is already—" she looked for the word, "—pointing in the right direction."

Marcus was quiet for a moment.

"Yes," he said.

"And?"

He looked around the room. Deren, who'd spent thirty years practicing a healing art without anyone giving them the tools to understand the foundation under it, and who had clearly been quietly furious about that without having anything to direct the fury at. Aldric, who'd hit the ceiling of his institution's knowledge base some time ago and had been trying not to notice. Senna, who was old enough and secure enough in herself to sit across from something that had made the entire architecture of her life's work look like a sketch and respond by pulling out a pencil and asking where to start. Cael, who'd spent years asking questions into a silence that had no answers to give back and had just kept asking anyway.

And the others — the ones outside, the messenger, the apprentices on the road. Everything the motes were still feeding back.

"It's not a shortage," he said. "That's the first thing I was trying to determine. I thought it might be — that something about this world, the culture or the magic system or the institutional structure, might have actively selected against this kind of thinking. Some worlds do that." He turned a page he wasn't reading. "It's not a shortage. The people who want to know why and not just how are here. They're just—"

"Isolated," Senna said.

"Isolated. And without language. That's the bigger problem." He looked at his hands. "If you don't have the words for something you can't share the thought. You can have the thought — people do, clearly — but it stays inside. Doesn't compound. Each person starts from zero instead of building on the last person's zero."

Mira, who had not spoken since she'd arrived, who had been sitting with her sleeping daughter and listening to everything with the quiet attention of someone deciding how much to trust what they were hearing, said:

"So what do you do about that."

Everyone looked at her.

She didn't seem to mind. "You've been deciding. Since before the city. You said so — you were deciding whether to help and whether help was even the right word." She shifted her daughter slightly against her shoulder. "You've made the decision. I can tell. So what is it."

Marcus looked at her for a long moment.

"Language first," he said. "Vocabulary for things that don't have names here. A framework people can use to share what they already know and build on it, instead of each person reinventing it alone." He paused. "Then institutions. Different ones from what's here — designed around the question rather than the answer. Places where the people like Cael and Deren can go to find each other."

"Schools," Aldric said.

"Schools," Marcus said, "that teach differently from your schools. Your schools teach the Fourfold Root. Mine would teach why someone built the Fourfold Root and what questions they were trying to answer and what questions they didn't think to ask."

Senna said: "That's a long project."

"Yes."

"You'd need to not be destroyed by the various entities who'd prefer the world stay as it is."

"Yes."

"And you'd need—" She stopped. Looked at him steadily. "You'd need people who could take what you know and translate it. Because what you know is several centuries ahead of what anyone here can receive directly. You need—" She seemed to be locating a word. "Intermediaries. People who understand enough of both to build bridges."

Marcus looked at her.

"That's what we are," she said. Not a question.

"That's what you could be," he said. "If you wanted."

The room was quiet.

Deren said: "I want."

Aldric said, almost simultaneously: "Yes."

Yara looked at her brother. Some communication that didn't need words. Cael gave a small shrug that meant something specific to both of them. She looked back at Marcus. "What does it involve, practically?"

"Learning things that will make you uncomfortable," Marcus said. "Being wrong frequently in the process. Carrying knowledge that will put you at odds with institutions that have a stake in the current model." He paused. "Also possibly moving, depending on how the next several hours go."

He glanced at the sound barrier. The throne room beyond it had reorganized. The advisors had coalesced into two visible factions. The clergy had done the same. The king was in conference with someone who had the bearing of a senior military commander and the expression of a man presenting options he didn't like.

"Speaking of which," Marcus said.

He let the sound barrier down.

The noise came back gradually — the room had gone quieter in the time they'd been insulated from it, the specific quiet of people who have exhausted immediate responses and moved into calculation.

The king looked at Marcus.

Marcus looked at the king.

For a moment nobody said anything on either side. Then the military commander — older, scarred, with the mana structure of someone who had done serious combat work for thirty years — stepped forward.

"What do you want," he said. Flat, direct. The question of a man who had decided that since none of the standard options were working he might as well try the non-standard one.

Marcus considered him. Good instincts. Right question.

"Seran," Marcus said. "Your turn."

Seran stood up and straightened and became, visibly, the person he was in his official capacity rather than around a campfire — not false, just formal, the difference between a tool in a drawer and a tool in a hand.

He began to talk.

Marcus leaned back in his chair and looked at the ceiling and went back to thinking about foundations. About the shape of a thing that didn't exist yet but could. About first drafts and how to revise them. About a world that was, underneath its surface, full of people asking questions into silence.

His right hand was moving slightly. Building something small and intricate and invisible in the air beside his chair, just to have something to do while he thought.

Cael was watching the movement of his fingers with bright, focused eyes.

Marcus noticed this without looking at him.

He adjusted the motion slightly — slowing it, making the structure visible, narrating nothing.

Just showing, the way you'd show someone who wanted to understand.

Cael leaned forward.

The negotiation took four hours.

Seran did most of the talking. Marcus did none of it. He sat in his chair and read and built small invisible things and occasionally confirmed a fact when Seran asked him to and once, at a crucial juncture when the conversation was about to break down over the question of whether the demon's continued presence in the city was a theological violation, he turned to the senior clergyman and asked him a single question about the foundational texts of his order that the clergyman could not answer, and then answered it himself, accurately, from memory, which produced a silence that Seran used effectively.

By the end there were terms. Provisional, fragile, burdened with conditions and caveats and an energy of mutual distrust that wasn't going away any time soon. But terms.

The king did not look at Marcus when they were concluded.

The military commander did. Brief, assessing. Marcus met the look and gave him nothing but presence, and the commander turned away with an expression that said the assessment was ongoing.

When the room had emptied down to their group and a small guard contingent that had been assigned to escort them to quarters — which Marcus was not going to use but didn't see a reason to say so yet — Seran sat back down heavily and put his face in his hands.

Mira put her free hand on his back.

"You did that well," Marcus said.

Seran looked up. "We have three weeks before someone changes their mind."

"Then we'll use three weeks." Marcus stood. The small invisible thing beside his chair was done — he held it up and looked at it. It was nothing significant, just a small self-sustaining mana structure in the shape of a problem he'd been thinking about, something to return to. He pocketed it. "There are things I want to look at in this city. People I want to find. I'll start tonight."

"The dungeons," Deren said.

A pause.

"The dungeons first," Marcus said. "That's not a variable I'll hold constant."

No one argued with this.

He walked toward the wall, and then through it in the sense that the wall rearranged its opinion about whether he was on one side or the other, and outside the castle the city was heading toward evening, full of its ten thousand lives, and Marcus began to move through it like a current, quiet and purposeful and leaving no mark on the surface.

Underneath the surface was a different matter.

He was already making notes.

Without looking, Marcus moves them all into orbs, time-frozen. A problem to be solved by experts in mind and body... Which is not his wheel house.

Marcus explains to the party that what changed his world almost overnight was two things.

Production being uncapped by machines and factories.
Knowledge being shared easily through the internet. 

He has the solution for both. He holds up the mote. The mote splits into two motes. The magic members of the party feels mana flowing into both motes. 

Where will our new city be, he asks, as a map unrolls itself in front of the party. He says to not be concerned about resources, land... Matter is matter. Within even a clump of dirt, limitless potential can be found. What we should be concerned about is how the factions of the world will react. How will others reach the city if they want.

Marcus says that defense isn't a concern too. Walls aren't even needed. If the party wants, he don't think they do, he could build the new city right above this current city. It'll probably cause a lot of problems, but it's not impossible. He's the problem solver.
