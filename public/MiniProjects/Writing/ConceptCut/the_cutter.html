A story of severance

The
Cutterand what remained whole

There were five of them when they entered the valley. The healer, who kept her faith wrapped tight like a wound. The archer, quiet to the point of near-disappearance. The mage, whose words cost something each time she spent them. The wanderer, whose name had been forgotten by everyone, himself included. And the swordsman, who had carried the same blade for eleven years and knew its weight better than he knew his own face.

They did not go looking for what found them. Nobody does, not really. The thing at the valley's far end had been waiting there since before the valley had a name, patient in the way that only old and terrible things can be patient. When they saw it, they all knew at once that they were probably going to die.

They fought anyway. That is the particular stubbornness of people who have traveled a long way together.

The healer ran dry first. Then the mage went silent, the cost of her last working still visible in the trembling of her hands. The archer's quiver was long empty, and the wanderer lay somewhere behind him in the dark, breathing in a way that sounded like a question.

The swordsman was the last one standing, and his sword was not.

It snapped at the hilt. He heard it before he felt it, a sound like a sentence interrupted. The enemy before him was still moving, still vast, still entirely indifferent to him. He looked at the broken handle in his hand. He looked at the air where the blade used to be.

He swung anyway.

Not from hope. Not from training. He swung because the motion had already begun somewhere deep in the hinge of his shoulder, already traveling down his arm, already committed to arrival, and stopping would have been the stranger act. The broken edge passed through nothing. Through air. Through the space where intention goes when there is nothing left to carry it.

Something opened. He felt it the way you feel a room change when someone quietly leaves it.

He did not understand what had happened. But the thing before him was no longer moving.

The mountain had been listening. Mountains are not passive, he had once been told by a very old woman who kept bees and seemed to have no reason to lie. They think slowly, in the way stone thinks, which is mostly about weight and time and the specific angle of everything. This mountain had been thinking its own long thought for perhaps two centuries, and it chose this moment to finish it.

The slope released itself along a clean geometric line, the way a hand opens when it finally lets go of something. It did not fall so much as slide, with a sound like the world clearing its throat. And as it moved, as the mass of it shifted and opened, the setting sun came through the gap it left, and the valley filled with the kind of light that has no business being beautiful but is.

He stood in it. The orange of it fell across his face and across the ground around him, and he became aware, slowly, of the state of the valley. The battle was over. The evidence was everywhere.

He closed his eyes. He kept them closed for a long time. When he opened them, the light had shifted a degree warmer, the sun another fraction down, and he understood that the world had decided to continue without asking him.

He walked back to the others. There was nothing else to do.

The months that followed were quiet in the way that things are quiet after great noise. He stayed in a small town the others had recommended, in a room above a cooperage where the sound of barrel-making came up through the floor all day, regular as breathing. He ate. He slept. He did not carry the broken sword.

Then one morning he picked it up again, and he began to think.

It had not been the steel. He had known this since the moment it happened, known it the way you know a thing you are not ready to examine. The blade had been a instrument of focus, a tuning fork held up to something that had always been vibrating inside him at the frequency of severance. When the blade disappeared, the frequency remained. The cut remained. It had simply needed to find a new way out.

The cut was never in the steel.
It was in the intention behind it.

He went into the hills alone, because some questions can only be asked in silence, and some answers need room to be strange without frightening anyone.

He began carefully, with the obvious things. He cut wood. He cut rope. He cut water, which did not stay cut but held the memory of parting for a moment before it forgot. He cut air, and felt it remember. He began to understand that everything had a seam, if you knew how to look.

Then he began to wonder about less obvious seams.

A spell in the moment between casting and arrival YES
A concept, the idea of fire, the notion of cold YES
Memory, the thread between a moment and its weight YES
The bond between a cause and its effect YES
A natural law. Gravity. The insistence of entropy. Time. YES. BUT.

He sat with that last answer for a long time before he did anything about it.

The first time he cut gravity, just a localized pocket, just a test, a flock of birds a hundred miles away briefly forgot which direction the sky was. They corrected themselves. Most of them.

The second time, he cut the rule that says fire requires fuel. It burned very brightly for three seconds, then remembered that it should not exist. In the moment of that confusion, everything within thirty feet smelled of smoke for the rest of his life, no matter how far he traveled from any fire.

The third time, he cut one second from time, a single removed moment between two consecutive instants, and for the rest of that day he kept finishing other people's sentences. Not from perception. He had simply already heard them.

The laws did not break. They were cut. And a cut thing heals wrong, the same as anything else. Scar tissue where there was once clean order. The world around him began to accumulate small wrongnesses in the places he had passed through: shadows at incorrect angles, doors opening onto rooms that were slightly the wrong size, conversations that looped once before completing, like a record that had learned to skip forward instead of back.

Nothing catastrophic. Just the universe, slowly growing scar tissue around the places where he had been.

He understood something else then, sitting in a room that was very slightly the wrong shape, in a chair that was almost certainly where he had left it.

Not: can I cut this.
But: what should remain whole.

He still carries the broken sword. He walks carefully now.
