Thank you for the feedback! 

To be honest, I could pull off the build itself from my lab for roughly $500, the hardware vision is almost locked in. What the residency represents is something different: a debut. It would be my first time out of the country, my first international showing, and honestly, a chance for me and Shawn to meet you in Copenhagen. Shawn's been enthusiastic about that idea, with him suggesting that this might lead to our third research paper together.

The core of the deck is what I'm calling an omnivorous black box: a rugged, indestructible portable computer that can run off practically anything. Hot rock by a campfire? Thermoelectric generators draw watts overnight. Car alternator? Just run cables. Handcrank, watermill, wind, scavenged DC and batteries the deck eats it all. The chassis philosophy is "dolphin flipper designed by an actual engineer": elegant, functional, and evolved for the environment rather than for the marketing brochure.

It'd be something similar to a NUC in a pelican case, with handles on both sides and a way for it to be hung off belts. It's easily deployed and easily stowed away in the field. A real nerd cowboy's revolver for surviving slightly longer during the apocalypse! 

What would be worth writing about is what lives on top of that core. I'm planning to layer in a real-time FFT-to-haptic system, exactly the kind of DHH accessibility research we've been working on, along with on-device speech-to-text accurate enough to embarrass my phone, all running locally on a machine that doesn't need a wall outlet to exist. 

Also, adding any synth-like features to the deck to fit the NEA, as Shawn suggested, would be trivial, like a movement sensor that translates my body movement into drums. Or a bit of computer vision to capture my fingers, translating sign language to drum beats! Imagination is the limit with a build like this.
