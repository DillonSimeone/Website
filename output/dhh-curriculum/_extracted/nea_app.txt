Organizational Background *
CymaSpace is a nonproﬁt, Deaf-led Disability Arts & Cultural Hub in Portland, Oregon with the mission of making
arts, culture and media accessible to the Deaf community through Technology, Education and Outreach. We promote
Deaf performers, train media technicians, mentor Disability Artists, and enable the diverse cultural communities that
intersect with Deafness to enjoy representation within our organization and at public events throughout Portland and
beyond.
CymaSpace is inspired by the opportunities that cymatics, the study of translating sound waves into visual and
tactile phenomena, provides to improve experiences for Deaf, DeafBlind, Hard of Hearing and hearing people. We
produce creative technology-driven media for live performances, artistic installations, and video recordings to “make
sound visible.” Physically, we enable access through Open Captioning displays, sound activated light systems, and
innovative multimedia art. Emotionally, we create opportunities for participants to feel deeply connected to works of

a. RESEARCH MOTIVATION.  
Research Question: How does a Deaf-centered STEAM music curriculum improve
engagement, learning outcomes, and creative expression for K-12 students,
particularly those who are Deaf or Hard-of-Hearing?
Music has an indelible presence, shaping emotions, culture, and experiences.
However, for the Deaf and Hard-of-Hearing (DHH), this profound avenue of human
expression remains largely inaccessible. Music serves as a conduit for emotional
resonance and social connection within the hearing-able world, aﬀording music-
makers and audiences with sonic agency [1]. Sonic Agency is the foundation of
CymaSpace’s Universal Music Design (UMD) project and transcends auditory
perception, advocating for the expansion of avenues through which all individuals
can engage with and experience music’s profound impact on the human psyche
[2]. This project aims to bridge the auditory richness of music with the tactile world,
using assistive technologies such as haptic vibration and immersive audio reactive
lighting that translates audio frequencies into color. These innovations provide a
novel means to tangibly experience the essence of music. Early music training
helps children develop equilibrium and proprioception, but DHH children do not
develop these skills in the same manner as a hearing-able child, and can struggle
with activities requiring balance, such as riding a bicycle [15].
In the K-12 school setting, despite facing marginalization from music education
activities [20], DHH music students succeed at learning music when given the
opportunity [23]. Additionally, accessibility research has an under-representation of
DHH designers, musicians, composers, and performers as active participants
throughout the design research phases [3]. This project will engage collaborators
from DHH and hearing populations and Cochlear Implant/Hearing Aid (CI/HA)
users.

This study seeks to answer the following research questions: (1) What educational
tools can be developed to increase multi-sensory music education in K-12
settings? (2) What are DHH student perceptions of their multisensory musical
experiences of a haptic curricular prototype? (3) What are DHH students’ attitudes
towards the use of the haptic curricular prototype in their multisensory musical
experiences? (4) Does a multi-sensory haptic curriculum improve music knowledge
and understanding for K-12 DHH students?
Through early phases of this investigation, UMD Team Leader, Nate Hergert
(Deaf/CI user) and UMD Lead Deaf Design Engineer Dillon Simeone have
developed and tested haptic technologies training themselves in music theory and
audio engineering concepts towards arriving at a platform for experimentation. As
this work requires a controlled acoustical environment, Hergert consulted with an
acoustical architect towards the acoustical analysis and design of the studio
purpose built for DHH audio and haptics research. 
The team has since developed a custom framework with novel interfaces and
haptics for Deaf music creation. Our ﬁrst interface GestoLumina (GeLu) builds
directly on the work of Advisory Board member Doga Cavdir [25], while
incorporating the UMD team’s background in both complex non-invasive, idiomatic
3D musical gesture tracking [26] and ASL gesture recognition [27]. This study will
be modeled after “Sonic Interaction Design” (MIT Press) [35], advised by Board
Member Stefania Seraﬁn, Ph.D., (editor of  “Sonic Interaction Design).
b. RESEARCH DESIGN.
We will develop a curricular prototype to promote sonic agency and improve music
education experiences and outcomes for K-12 DHH students. This curriculum will
incorporate haptic vibration devices to translate sound for DHH students and will
be tested for impact on their ability to engage with a music education curriculum.
This qualitative study involves a triangulation of approaches including surveys,
interviews, and observations in both a laboratory and practical setting. 
Based on early exploratory workshops with DHH students, it is anticipated that the
results of this study will provide valuable information for engineers on the eﬃcacy
of the designed haptic devices incorporated into the curricular approach for
including DHH students with multisensory musical experiences in the school
classroom [64] as well as inform educators of the DHH in specialist and
mainstream schools on ways to include DHH students in a music-centric STEM
education [65], with implications for curriculum design and implementation [66].

I. Sample(s).
Our control group will consist of: Nate Principal Investigator (Cochlear Implant
User), Myles (Hearing Aid User), Shawn (Hearing), and Dillon (totally Deaf).
Inclusion criteria for research group students include proﬁciency in reading and
writing English and ﬂuency in American Sign Language (ASL). Exclusion criteria
involve any medical condition in which multisensory stimulation might lead to
distress in the student, such as hypersensitivity. We expect to study a maximum of
50 students across all programs. 
Participants in the study will engage in up to three test occurrences over up to
three days. Each test occurrence will include: one standard curricular music
education experience, such as a demonstration of music scales on a piano or
guitar, without intervention; the same music education experience with the
intervention of engineer-designed haptic devices [84]; followed by one of three
research instruments. Each occurrence will take no longer than the length of a
typical classroom lesson (up to 60 minutes). In total, each participating student will
participate in up to three test occurrences.
Researchers will collect three sets of information from each student. First, the
researchers will conduct a survey with open-ended questions, where students will
record their perceptions of their musical experience as impacted by the inclusion
of speciﬁc haptic devices in classroom music education activities. Questions will
be written in a style that is appropriate for participants 13 and older. Students will
be given appropriate lengths of time to complete their responses to each activity.
The overall experience will be designed to be completed within one typical lesson
period of the normal school day. Second, researchers will work with students
individually in a smaller oﬃce space, on a diﬀerent day, where similar activities will
be oﬀered and ﬁeld notes will be taken by the researcher to record observed
behaviors. Third, researchers will conduct scripted interviews with the same
students individually using open-ended questions. Questions will again be
designed in a style that is appropriate to the age group of the participants. The
overall experience of the observation and interview will be designed to be
completed within one typical lesson period of the normal school day. All three sets

Research Procedure: By mutual agreement between the school administrators,
relevant faculty, and the researcher, an oral presentation will be given to students
introducing them to the study. Parental consent and student assent will be
obtained in writing on the form prior to the start of the study. Students meeting
inclusion criteria will be presented with a range of educational musical
experiences. The researcher will take ﬁeld notes on observed student responses
and will follow with the interview, asking students scripted questions about their
experiences that will aim to obtain more detailed information than was achieved in
the survey alone. 
II. Data Source(s). 
Data collection will be accomplished through three sources: surveys written by
students at the Oregon School for the Deaf or other participating institution(s),
researcher ﬁeld notes written during observations of the same students, and
transcripts of audio recordings of student responses to interviews. Because audio
recordings of the interview responses will have identifying voice recognition data,
the recordings will be destroyed immediately after transcription. Data will be coded
to respect anonymity of students through data anonymization, randomization, and
masking using Python using an in-house engineer. Data will be stored on the
researcher’s encrypted computer that is password protected. Data will be
destroyed within ﬁve years after the study is complete. The data is limited to this
speciﬁc sample of participants. It is further limited by the capacity of the students
to express the nature of their experiences in written and oral communications.
Balance to the self-reporting will be achieved through the observations of facial
and gestural behaviors of students by the researcher during the musical
experiences with and without the haptic devices. There will be a pause after each
experience to allow the researcher to make notes. The researcher will follow a
study-speciﬁc protocol to remain objective and reduce bias while noting student
responses during observations. When participants are recruited, and when the
study is administered, the importance of accuracy in responses will be explained
as well as the fact of there being no right, wrong, or preferred answers. While each
new experience will be presented both with and without the haptic devices, the
inclusion or exclusion of the haptic device will occur in random order each time.  
These data will inform the impact that haptic device interventions have on DHH
students when learning music. Anticipated limitations posed by the data include a
reliance on DHH students ﬂuent in ASL who can also read and write in an
additional language, English. This may bias the sample toward DHH students with
greater exposure and integration in the hearing world than DHH students who
primarily operate within the Deaf culture.
Routine monitoring and oversight includes quarterly reporting to and review by our
expert Advisory Board, with increased oversight occurring in the weeks prior to,
during, and following our research with live research subjects.

c. DATA ANALYSIS.
Once surveys, ﬁeld notes, and interview transcripts have been collected, the
analysis of qualitative data will commence. Data will be coded and entered into a
computer database. All coding of data and allocation of data categories will follow
an emergent design because the researcher expects to see unanticipated answers
and views expressed by the students. All data will be analyzed for trends and
discrepancies in the results. Each researcher will write a narrative report, and a
discussion of those results will follow. Assuming a methodological assumption,
categories will be assigned during the analysis of data to allow for new categories
to emerge that might not have been envisaged in advance.
