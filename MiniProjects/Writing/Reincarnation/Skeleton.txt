The first people arrived within a month.

Most people who heard this nodded and went back to their lives. Institutions heard it and began their slow machinery of threat assessment. A specific kind of person heard it, the kind who had been lying awake asking questions into silence, and was on the road within the week.

Deren received them.

This had not been planned. It had emerged from the first month of the city's existence, during which the party had been collectively figuring out what they were for in a place that mostly ran itself — the production mote handled manufacture, the knowledge mote was building its index, the infrastructure maintained without maintenance — and Deren had simply been the person standing near the entrance when the first arrivals came through, and had been good at it, and had kept doing it.

A child, eleven years old, alone, who had walked for two weeks and when asked why had said: I heard there was a place where they answer questions.

The questions, it turned out, were about plants — specifically about why plants grew differently in different soils, which was a question with a long and complex and beautiful answer that the child had independently begun to construct from observation without knowing it was a question that had occupied serious minds for centuries in another world.

Her name was Pell.

The vocabulary came from Marcus, mostly indirectly — he sent up written materials from the ocean floor with a regularity that suggested he'd been preparing them for a long time, covering the foundational principles of how mana actually functioned at the substrate level, stripped of the institutional language that had accumulated over centuries on top of the actual mechanics. No Fourfold Roots, no invocations, no ceremonial framework. Just: here is what is happening. Here is why it works.

The arguments that produced the curriculum were about sequence — what you taught first, how you built the scaffold, which intuitions you wanted students to have before they encountered the formalisms. The first cohort of students was twelve people ranging in age from fourteen to sixty.

The error, Cael came to understand, was one the previous world had made continuously and at significant cost.

Seran spent the first year solving problems.

Someone had to. The city ran itself in the sense that Marcus had built the infrastructure to run without administration, but a city was not only infrastructure. A city was the people in it and the disputes between them and the questions of how things were shared and who decided when the rules needed to change.

Seran was not a politician. He thought of himself as a soldier, and he was right, but he was a specific kind of soldier — the kind who had spent years making decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information and caring a great deal about outcomes for people he was responsible for. This turned out to be most of the skill set required.

Mira did the other half.

She had a mind for systems, specifically for the places where systems failed — where the theory of how something should work diverged from how it actually ran, where the gap between intention and outcome was quietly producing harm that nobody was looking at directly. She found these gaps the way a tongue finds a sore tooth, persistently, without being asked to.

They made it visible in the architecture. The central meeting space had no permanent seat of authority — the room could be reconfigured, the focal point moved, the implication of hierarchy redistributed depending on what the meeting was for. A market stall was, stripped to fundamentals, a daily exercise in reading what people needed, what they were afraid of, what they'd accept and what they'd refuse, and how to offer something new in the language of something familiar. He understood that the world outside the city was not going to become the city quickly, and that the gap between the two was not a problem to be solved but a relationship to be managed, and that managing it badly was how the city became a target.

He became, without the title being formally given, the person who handled the outside.

The king sent an envoy in the second year.

She stayed.

The kelp moved in the current above him. The surface light rippled and shifted with the movement of the water. A fish investigated his window with the incurious investigation of something that had decided he was not food and therefore not particularly interesting.

He thought about how the city was already doing this, quietly, for more people than he was directly tracking.

Teaching, he thought, with the slight internal resistance of someone identifying a word that was correct but personally uncomfortable.

He was, apparently, teaching.

Outside, the kelp forest moved in its slow enormous way. The light came down from the surface in long angled shafts, green and shifting and ancient, illuminating nothing in particular and everything in it.

The city hummed, forty meters above, and continued to become what it was going to become.

Marcus kept writing.

Part Twelve: The Ocean Floor

They took a boat to where he'd told them, a point half a mile offshore where the water was deep enough that the bottom wasn't visible, and Seran said the words Marcus had given them.

The water opened.

Not dramatically — no parting of waves, no theatre. A path simply became available that hadn't been before, a pressure adjustment that let the boat descend without the ocean noticing, and they went down.

Yara was the first to stop pretending to be composed.

She'd fought in campaigns. She'd seen the city Marcus built appear from nothing over the course of a week. She'd watched him fold space with a piece of paper and put an entire castle into a sphere the size of a melon. She thought she had a working model of what to expect from him.

The ocean floor was not in her model.

He had not built a house.

He had, at some point in the past two years, built something that had started as a house and then kept going in the way his projects kept going — each solution revealing an adjacent problem, each structure suggesting the next one, the whole thing accreting into something that had its own internal logic and beauty and was answering questions she couldn't entirely formulate.

The kelp forest was part of it, integrated rather than cleared. The structures moved between and around the great columns of kelp the way a city moves around its old trees — accommodating, incorporating, treating the existing life as infrastructure rather than obstacle. Light came from everywhere and nowhere, bioluminescent in quality, making the whole space feel like the inside of something that was alive and thinking.

The mote-entities were everywhere.

She recognized them from the city — the same quality of deliberate distributed attention, the same sense of things being managed that didn't need managing in ways that freed everything else from having to think about them. Down here they were more numerous and more complex and moved with a kind of expressiveness that the ones in the city didn't have. They passed through the water like slow fish. They gathered at certain points in configurations that shifted like long thoughts.

"What are those doing," Aldric said. His voice had the specific quality it had in the curriculum sessions when he'd encountered something that required new vocabulary.

"Thirteen different things," Marcus said, from somewhere to their left. "Presently."

He was sitting in a space that was approximately a workshop and approximately a library and approximately neither — a room where the distinction between working and thinking had been dissolved by someone who didn't experience them as different activities. Papers everywhere, physical ones, covered in notation that wasn't any magical script Aldric recognized. Things in various stages of construction or deconstruction. A window — a large one, occupying an entire wall — looking out into the kelp at a depth where the light went very blue and very slow.

He looked, to the party's slight collective surprise, good.

Not transformed, not dramatically different. But there was something in how he was sitting — a looseness, an ease — that they hadn't seen before. Two years of living at the bottom of the ocean had apparently agreed with him in some specific way.

"The kingdoms have combined," Seran said.

"I know." He said it without looking up from what he was writing. "Eighty-seven thousand soldiers currently. They're arguing about supply chains at the moment. The march will start within the month."

"You've been watching."

"I've been watching." He finished a notation, set it aside. "I've also been thinking about it, which is more useful." He gestured at the seating — chairs appeared with the same ambient certainty as always. "Sit down. You look like you're delivering bad news and I'd like you to be comfortable when you realize you're not."

They sat.

Deren was the last one, because Deren was standing at the window looking at the kelp forest with the expression they got when encountering something that required extended processing.

"This is the real one," Deren said, not quite to anyone.

"What is," Yara asked.

"The miracle." Deren turned from the window. "The city up there — I've been living in it two years, I've stopped seeing it properly. It's become— familiar. But this—" They looked at the workshop, the motes, the library-that-wasn't-a-library, the window looking into the oldest dark. "This is what's actually been happening."

Marcus looked at Deren for a moment. "I've been busy," he said, which was, everyone in the room recognized, the complete and honest account of the past two years.

"With what," Aldric said.

"Later," Marcus said. "Army first."

He was, Seran noticed, in a good mood. Not a performing-good-mood — a genuine ease, an undercurrent of something that was almost enthusiasm, which was new. Marcus's baseline was focused, not cheerful. Whatever the army problem was doing to his internal state, it was not what Seran had expected.

"Options," Marcus said, and held up a hand and began to count.

The first option he described flatly and efficiently: seal them all into orbs, let the city's justice system handle them one by one, problem contained indefinitely.

The second he described with similar efficiency: the city does nothing, the approaching army finds the horizon receding as they march, travel time stretching without apparent cause, they eventually run out of road and go home.

He described the third — the barriers, the impermeability, ignoring the army the way you ignore weather — with the tone of someone presenting a sound if uninspired solution.

Then he got to the fourth.

Something shifted in the way he was sitting.

He still wasn't performing. But there was an energy in how he started describing it — a particular quality of focus that the party had learned to recognize as Marcus talking about something he'd already thought about in more detail than the current conversation required.

Golems, he said. Ninety thousand of them, roughly, to match numbers. A factory running overnight, fed matter from the ocean and the air — and there is so much matter in air, people here still don't appreciate this — producing units that were large enough to be unarguable and gentle enough to be absurd.

They wouldn't fight. Fighting implied a conflict on terms both parties recognized. These would simply — he paused here, choosing the word with the care of someone who wanted the exact right one — collect. One soldier at a time. Lift them carefully, carry them home, set them down on ground they recognized, leave.

"The journey home would take some time," he said. "So naturally the units would be equipped to make it comfortable. Water, food — good food, not field rations — waste processing so no one is degraded by the experience." He paused again. "Books."

"Books," Seran said.

"In the relevant languages. A selection. History, natural philosophy, some fiction, practical texts. People have time on a long journey. Some of them will read." He looked at his hands. "Some of them won't. But it's there."

The room was quiet for a moment.

"You've already designed them," Yara said.

Marcus did not confirm or deny this. He looked at the window.

"It would be embarrassing for the kingdoms," he said. "That's the consideration. Ninety thousand soldiers collected and returned home by golems carrying packed lunches and reading material. "I'm not certain if that escalates things further or ends them. I've been running the variables and I keep getting different answers depending on assumptions about human psychology I can't fully verify."

"It would be humiliating," Seran said.

"Yes."

"Deeply, specifically humiliating in a way that a conventional defeat isn't."

"Yes. A defeat can be explained. A defeat has dignity, even in loss. This—" He looked at the ceiling, the ocean above it, something further than that. "This would be a story people told for a hundred years. The golem army that carried the soldiers home. The commanders who got picked up and walked back like they'd wandered somewhere they shouldn't be."

"You want to do it," Deren said.

A pause.

"I find it very funny," Marcus said, with the precise honesty of someone who has decided to stop pretending to be more dignified than they are about something. "In my previous world they called them robots. I spent my career working with systems that did work so humans didn't have to. What I always—" He stopped. A very slight change in his expression, the kind that meant something had surfaced that didn't usually surface. "In my world the problem was always that the people in charge decided what the machines did, and what the machines did was usually— more of the same things humans had been doing, just faster. Extraction. Production for accumulation. The same patterns, amplified." He looked at his hands again. "Ninety thousand units that do nothing but gently prevent harm and ensure comfort and carry soldiers home to their families. That's—"

He stopped again.

"That's what I always wanted to point the tools at," he said. "And nobody ever let me."

The room was very quiet.

Marcus appeared to notice that he'd said more than he'd intended. He looked at the window.

"There's a castle," he said, and his tone shifted in a way the party had never heard before — lighter, slightly, almost reminiscent. "From a story in my world. A moving castle. Mechanical, magical, runs on a fire demon's contract, walks on legs across a landscape." He turned a pen in his hand. "I always thought there was something— the image of it. Something large and strange and self-contained, moving through a world that didn't fully understand it, belonging to no territory, beholden to no king." He was quiet for a moment. "The structural engineering would be interesting. Something that size, ambulatory, the joint load calculations alone—"

He caught himself.

The party watched him catch himself.

It was, Aldric thought, like watching someone realize they'd started talking about something they loved before they'd decided whether they wanted to.

"The golems," Marcus said, returning to the thread with deliberate precision. "That's the option I'd choose. But I want to be clear: it should be the city's choice. I have a bias here that I've just made somewhat visible."

"Somewhat," Yara said.

"The castle," Mira said. She had that look — the systems look, the finding-the-gap look. "You were designing it just now. In your head."

"I was thinking about load calculations."

"While explaining your army options to us."

"I can think about more than one thing."

"How long have you been thinking about the castle."

A pause that was approximately one beat too long.

"Intermittently," Marcus said.

The party exchanged a look. The kind of look that passes between people who have been through enough together that it carries full sentences.

Seran said: "What if the castle went to meet the army."

Marcus looked at him.

"Before the golems," Seran said. "A moving castle, on the horizon. Not threatening. Just— present. Something that doesn't fit any category they have." He leaned forward. "Let them look at it for a while."

"And then," Yara said, with the particular tone of someone laying down a card, "let the golems come out of it."

Marcus was very still.

"With the books," Deren said.

"And the food," Mira added.

"And the waste processing," Cael-the-trader said, who had come along and had been quiet in the way he was quiet, taking everything in, until the right moment. "You want to send them home. Send them home well. Make the whole thing—" He thought about it. "Make it a story they tell that ends with and we were treated better by the machines than we were by our own commanders."

Marcus looked at them all.

The workshop was very quiet except for the ambient sound of the ocean, which was immense and constant and didn't care about any of this.

"The structural load calculations for ambulatory architecture of that scale," Marcus said, "are not trivial."

"No," Seran agreed.

"It would take approximately—" He was already doing it, they could see him doing it, the slight defocus of someone running numbers behind their eyes. "Three weeks for something structurally sound. Two if I compromise on certain—" He paused. "I won't compromise. Three weeks."

"We have four before they march," Seran said.

"Then it's not a time constraint problem."

"No," Seran said. "It's not."

Marcus looked at the window. The kelp moved in its long slow way outside. A mote drifted past, doing one of its thirteen things.

"The books," he said. "The selection matters. What they read on the way home matters."

"I'll work on it," Aldric said immediately.

"The food should be good," Marcus said. "Genuinely good. Not impressive, not performative. Good." He turned to Mira. "You understand the difference."

"I do," Mira said.

"And the golems—" He picked up a pen. "They shouldn't be frightening. They should be—" He paused, reaching for something. "They should look like they were built by something that thought very carefully about what a person needs and built that, without any interest in harm. The shape of genuine care expressed through engineering." He looked at the pen. "That's an aesthetic problem as well as a structural one."

"Yes," Deren said.

"I haven't done much aesthetic problem solving."

"I think," Deren said carefully, "that you might be better at it than you think."

Marcus looked at the workshop around him. The way the kelp forest had been incorporated rather than cleared. The bioluminescent light that fell like the ocean was thinking. The window that took up the whole wall because whoever had put it there had understood that the view was the point.

He said nothing.

He put pen to paper.

In the upper left corner of a fresh sheet, he sketched a leg. The joint structure. Where the load transferred. The question of how something that size moved without shaking itself apart.

The party watched him begin, and understood that the decision had been made, and that something was going to walk across the world's horizon in three weeks and change what the word castle meant for everyone who saw it.

Somewhere above them, the city hummed.

Marcus drew.

Part Thirteen: The Walk

It came over the hill at dawn.

The forward scouts saw it first.

One of them rode back to camp at a speed that caused his commander to stand up from breakfast and put down the cup he was holding. The scout was a twelve-year veteran. He had the face of someone who had categorized most things the world could show him and had made peace with the remainder.

He did not have that face anymore.

The army had camped in the wide valley two days' march from the coast, eighty-nine thousand soldiers in the organized sprawl of a serious military operation — supply lines, command structure, the whole weight of six kingdoms' combined political will made physical and pointed in one direction. It had taken eight months to assemble. Commanders who had spent careers learning not to be impressed by things were, quietly, impressed by the logistics of it. This was real. This was the kind of force that ended things.

The sun came up.

The castle came over the hill.

It was the legs first.

That was what everyone would say afterward, when there was an afterward and people were trying to explain it to people who hadn't been there. Not the size, though the size was — the size was something you felt before you understood it, a change in the quality of the air. Not the sound, though there was a sound, a deep rhythmic percussion that you understood after a moment was footsteps, which was the point at which most soldiers' faces did the thing the scout's face had done. The legs first. Because legs meant walking, and walking meant it was going somewhere, and the somewhere it was going was here.

It walked like it had always been walking and would always be walking. Not threatening — this was what the senior mage attached to the western coalition would report, with the frustrated precision of someone trying to describe something that kept escaping language. It didn't move like a weapon. A weapon moved toward something. This moved the way weather moved: with complete indifference to what was in its way, not hostile, just going.

It stopped at the valley's edge.

It looked at the army, if something that large could be said to look. There were no visible eyes. But the attention was palpable.

Then the doors opened.

They came out in rows.

Not marching — they didn't march, which was one of the details that lived in soldiers' accounts for the rest of their lives. Marching was a human thing, a trained thing, the thing you did to show force through coordination. These moved with the unhurried individual purpose of something that had a specific task and no interest in performing readiness.

They were taller than men. Not by enough to be monstrous — by enough to be unambiguous. The material was something between metal and stone and neither, a warm grey that caught the early light and didn't glare. The faces were — the faces were the part that the mage spent four pages trying to describe. Not human, not inhuman in the way of a mask. Somewhere else. Designed, clearly, with care, by something that had thought about what a face was for and built that without copying the specific human implementation.

They looked, the mage eventually concluded, like something that was interested in you without wanting anything from you. He had never encountered that combination before. He didn't have a word for it.

They spread into the valley in rows that were not military rows — not a formation, not a front. Something more like — he paused over this one for a long time — something more like the pattern of people moving through a market. Purposeful, distributed, each one going somewhere specific.

Then each one arrived somewhere specific.

The first soldier collected was a pikeman from the eastern coalition's third regiment.

He was nineteen. He had enlisted because the pay was better than the farm. He had been thinking about the farm, actually, when the golem reached him — thinking about whether the barley would be up yet, whether his brother had managed the drainage problem in the low field.

The golem stopped in front of him.

He looked up at it.

It looked at him, in its way.

Then it bent down — smoothly, with an unhurried care that was somehow the most alarming thing it had done — and picked him up.

Not roughly. Not the way you pick up something that doesn't matter. The way you pick up something fragile, with attention to the whole of it, making sure nothing was compressed wrong, nothing stressed. He was nineteen and large and it held him like he was made of something worth protecting.

He didn't have time to do anything. He also found, he would tell people later, that he didn't want to. Which he couldn't explain. He tried for years and couldn't explain it. It hadn't felt like surrender. It had felt like — something else. Something that didn't have a word yet in this world's language.

The golem straightened.

It turned.

It began to walk home.

Around it, eighty-eight thousand, nine hundred and ninety-nine similar conversations were occurring simultaneously.

The army did not go quietly — this is important, this is part of the record. Soldiers fought. The western coalition's cavalry charged. The senior mage deployed three separate area-suppression constructs that were, individually, the most powerful things he'd built in thirty years of practice.

The golems did not respond to any of this.

Not in the way of something that couldn't be harmed, though they couldn't be harmed. In the way of something that had been given a task and had assessed the other things happening as not the task. The cavalry charge moved around them the way water moves around something fixed. The area-suppression constructs washed over them and registered, apparently, as weather. A golem that was in the process of picking up a soldier got hit by a sword three times while completing the motion. It completed the motion.

The soldier got picked up.

The golem turned.

The golem walked home.

The soldier was still holding his sword. He kept holding it the whole way. He wasn't sure why.

The chest opened on the third hour.

Every golem had one — a compartment at the torso, the soldiers had noticed them but assumed weapons, assumed threat, and then the chest opened and inside was a container of water, sealed, clean, cold in a way that water left in metal shouldn't be. And wrapped things that turned out to be food. Real food. Not rations.

Bread that was still warm.

The pikeman from the eastern coalition, who had been trying to decide whether to be more angry or more frightened for three hours, unwrapped the bread and smelled it.

He ate it.

He was nineteen and he'd been marching for two weeks on field rations and it was good bread, warm, with something in it he couldn't identify that was either a spice or an herb or something that had no name in his vocabulary.

He ate the whole thing.

The golem walked.

Later there was cheese. Later still, something hot in a sealed container that opened at a temperature that was exactly right for eating, which should have been impossible for something that had been walking for hours and was probably the detail that finally broke the senior mage's attempt to write a rational account of the day.

The books appeared on the second day.

Another chest, another compartment, and inside it a selection of texts in the soldier's native language — which meant the golems knew who each of them was, which was a thought that took a while to process and then became strangely less alarming than it should have been, because if they'd known who you were and they'd made you warm bread, maybe the knowing wasn't the threat you'd have assumed.

Most soldiers didn't read them immediately.

Some did.

The pikeman from the eastern coalition read one on the third day because he'd run out of things to think about and the alternative was thinking about how he was being carried home by something he couldn't explain, which was not a productive thought loop.

The book was a history. Not the history he'd been taught — not the history of kings and battles and the assertions of power that accumulated into what his world called the past. A history of questions. How people had figured things out. What they'd been wrong about first, and why they'd been wrong, and what had happened when someone asked whether the wrongness was necessary.

He read it in two days.

He asked the golem for another one.

The chest opened.

He wasn't sure, afterward, if the golem had understood the request or if the chest opened on a schedule. He decided it didn't matter. He read the second book.

On the seventh day the western coalition commander was set down on a hillside two miles from the city his forces had marched from.

He stood up.

He looked at the golem.

The golem looked at him, in its way.

Then it turned, and walked back toward the coast, and joined the line of other golems returning to wherever golems went when they'd finished their task, and in twenty minutes they were specks on the horizon and in thirty they were gone.

The commander stood on the hill.

Around him, over the course of the next few hours, the rest of his forces were set down — on hills, in fields, on the roads they'd marched out on. Each one put down carefully, the same way they'd been picked up. Each one left standing on ground they recognized.

Nobody was injured.

The army that had taken eight months to assemble stood in the landscape of home and looked at each other and could not find the word for what had happened to them.

The mage wrote his report in four drafts.

The first draft was an attempt to describe what he'd seen accurately, which failed because the language didn't have the necessary concepts.

The second draft was an attempt to explain it, which failed because he didn't know enough to explain it.

The third draft was an attempt to assess the threat, which failed because the event had been, by any coherent measure, the opposite of a threat, and he couldn't work out how to frame the opposite of a threat from an entity of overwhelming power in terms that the council would receive as intelligence rather than philosophy.

The fourth draft he wrote honestly, which was the one he sent, and which ended:

I cannot tell you what this means for the war. I can tell you that of the twelve men in my immediate unit, ten read the books they were given. I read mine. I have been trying since to determine whether this was intentional on the part of whoever built those golems, and I find that it does not change anything whether it was or not. The question is the same question. I intend to find someone who can help me answer it. I believe I know where to look.

He did not wait for a response.

He was on the road north within the week.

The castle walked back to the coast and into the ocean.

Not dramatically. It stepped off the shoreline the way you step off a curb, with the same absolute ease it had moved across the valley, and the water rose to meet it and then it was gone, and the surface of the sea was undisturbed, and the morning was very quiet.

A fisherman who had been watching from his boat sat for a long time with his net in his lap.

Then he picked up the oars and rowed to shore and went home and told his wife.

She had heard stranger things this past year, she said.

She hadn't, actually. But she understood that he needed to say it out loud and she understood that some things you could only begin to hold by saying them, and she poured him something warm and sat down across from him and listened.

At the bottom of the ocean, Marcus was already thinking about something else.

Not dismissively — the golems had been filed under resolved, monitor for secondaries, the same category the castle was filed under now that the aesthetic problem was solved. He had a working model of the political cascade. Cael-the-trader was better placed to manage the immediate aftermath. The mage who was on the road north was going to be interesting to talk to.

He was thinking about the anomaly.

He'd been building around it for two years without quite building at it — circling, approaching, taking readings from different angles, the way you approach something sensitive when you know that how you approach it will determine what you're able to find. He'd been wrong about what it was twice. He'd been closer the second time.

He picked up his pen.

The kelp moved.

The light came down from the surface in its long slow shafts, green and ancient and patient.

He started writing.

Outside, somewhere, the world was telling itself a story about golems and warm bread and a walking castle and the army that came home intact and confused and, in some cases, reading. In six months it would be told in taverns in eight languages. In a year it would have acquired details that hadn't happened. In a generation it would be the kind of story parents told children, the details shifting with each telling into whatever shape the teller needed it to have.

The pikeman from the eastern coalition would eventually make his way to the city.

He would arrive with the book he'd been given, which he'd read four times, and a list of questions it had generated, which he'd been adding to since the seventh day of being carried home.

He would become, in a decade, one of the better teachers the city produced.

He would specialize in history — specifically, in the history of questions. How people had figured things out. What they'd been wrong about first. What had happened when someone finally asked whether the wrongness was necessary.

He would teach this to hundreds of students over forty years.

He would always begin the first class the same way: by putting a book on the table and telling the story of where it had come from, and watching the students' faces while he told it, and letting the story do its first work before the curriculum did its work, because some things had to be true in the body before they could be true in the mind.

He didn't know any of this yet.

He was on a hillside, holding his sword, watching the golems walk away.

Somewhere to the north, a city hummed.

At the bottom of the ocean, something was being figured out.

The world turned.
