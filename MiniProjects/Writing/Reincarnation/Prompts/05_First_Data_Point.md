# Prompt for First Data Point

Marcus looked at the fire.

He had not actually thought about this. He had been thinking forward so consistently and with such focus that backward had largely gone unexamined, which was typical of how he operated but perhaps not entirely healthy, he dimly recognized.

"Loud," he said. "Very loud. A lot of people. A lot of information, all the time." He turned his hand over, looking at the grey-black skin of Grak's fingers, which were his fingers now. "I didn't have many people I was close to there. I didn't really notice. I had work."

"Do you miss it?" Deren asked.

A genuine question. Not a trap, not a test. Just someone trying to understand what was sitting across the fire from them.

"I miss the infrastructure," Marcus said. "The accumulated knowledge. Libraries that held everything humans had ever figured out. Tools built on top of other tools going back centuries." He paused. "I don't miss being about to retire into doing nothing. That was — I don't think I actually wanted that. I think I wanted the problem of how to get there to be solved."

Seran said, quietly: "You were building toward an ending."

"I was optimizing toward a local maximum," Marcus said. "Which is a different thing."

Seran seemed to think about this. He had a good mind, Marcus had decided — not analytically inclined the way Aldric was, but broadly intelligent in the practical way of someone who had survived things by paying attention. "And now?"

"Now there's no local maximum in sight." He looked at the fire. Something that wasn't quite a smile moved across Grak's face, which was his face now. "That's better, I think."

He let them ask for another two hours. Aldric asked most of the questions, but the good ones kept coming from Deren and occasionally from Seran in the focused concise way Seran had of saying a lot with few words. Yara mostly listened with her eyes sharp and her assessment of everything ongoing.

Eventually, without quite deciding to, Marcus reached into the space above the fire and opened a window.

Not a spell. Not a construct. Just a projection — memory and mana structured into image, held in the air below where they could all see it.

A planet, seen from above. Blue and green and white.

"This is where I came from," he said.

They went quiet.

The planet turned slowly. He hadn't meant to animate it but the mana had a fidelity instinct by now, so it moved the way he remembered it moving, which was to say with the sublime slow indifference of something enormous and indifferent to being looked at.

He zoomed. Continent. Then country — he picked the coast, familiar geography. Then city.

The road structure came into view first. Then the density. Then the buildings, and he heard Yara inhale, and then the skyscrapers came into view and nobody said anything at all.

He let them look.

The city pulsed with its ten million lives. Lights and movement and the incomprehensible layered complexity of a civilization that had spent two hundred years industrializing and a hundred more after that building things on top of the industrialization. Bridges. Airports. Container ships in the harbor like grey islands with their own ecosystems of machinery.

Seran finally said: "Those towers."

"Buildings. People live and work in them."

"How many people."

"In this city, maybe ten million. The world held around eight billion when I left."

He watched them process the number. It didn't really land — he understood that. Eight billion was not a number human cognition handled gracefully. You'd need a different intuition for scale than most people developed, and even then it was more label than feeling.

"How did they—" Aldric started.

"Slowly. Over a very long time. And then all at once." Marcus looked at the image. "The magic there is different. It's not mana. It's physics — forces, materials, energy. They learned to read the rules of the physical world and exploit them systematically." He paused. "The results look similar from far enough out."

He closed the projection.

The campfire was very quiet for a moment.

Then Aldric said, in a voice that was carefully neutral in the way that means the speaker is not feeling neutral at all: "After seeing that. After telling us all of this. You understand what you're going to do to this world."

Marcus looked at him.

"By just existing," Aldric said. "By talking to us. By touching the mana where we can watch your hands." He gestured at himself, at the others, at the road behind them. "We're going to go back. We're going to tell people. And you're— what you know is going to—" He stopped. "This world is going to become unrecognizable."

"Yes," Marcus said.

"You knew that."

"I knew that."

"And you walked out of the castle anyway."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. The fire crackled. The feedback loops he'd built maintained their temperature differential with quiet efficiency.

"The alternative was staying in a castle full of demons waiting for something interesting to happen," he said. "That wasn't a serious option."

Seran looked at the road ahead, and then at Marcus, with the expression of a man recalculating a risk he thought he'd already assessed. "You've decided that changing the world is worth doing."

"I've decided it's probably going to happen regardless," Marcus said. "Given that, I'd rather be involved in how."

Nobody had a good answer to that. He hadn't expected one.
