# Around the Fire

He built the campfire wrong, by the standards of everyone watching.

Not wrong in the sense of ineffective — the fire was warm and stable and would burn for exactly as long as needed without consuming any additional fuel. Wrong in the sense that he'd structured the mana underneath it the way he structured everything, which meant the party could see the geometry of it if they looked at the flame from the right angle. Little recursive loops maintaining temperature. A pressure differential keeping smoke away from faces. Tiny feedback systems correcting for wind before the wind arrived.

The hero's hand had finally left his sword somewhere around the second hour of walking. That felt like progress.

"You have questions," Marcus said. It wasn't really an observation. He'd been watching them not ask things for several miles.

The caster spoke first. His name was Aldric — Marcus had gathered this from the others referring to him, not from any introduction — and he had the specific energy of someone who has been holding a question so long it had started to hurt.

"The thing you built in the corridor," Aldric said. "The—" he moved his hands trying to describe it. "The mote. The entity."

"What about it."

"It adapted. I've never seen— adaptation isn't a property spells have. A spell is a structure. It's fixed. You construct it, you release it, it does the thing it does." He was leaning forward. "What you built wasn't doing the thing it did. It was deciding what to do."

Marcus looked at the fire for a moment. "You're describing the difference between a script and a program."

"I don't know what those words mean."

"A script is a fixed sequence of instructions. A program is a set of rules for generating instructions based on conditions." He paused. "Your spells are scripts. I was curious what would happen if I tried to write a program instead."

Aldric stared at him. Then he sat back slowly, like a man who has been punched gently enough that he's not sure if it was a punch.

"That's all it was?" he said. "You were curious?"

"Most things I've built started that way."

A silence around the fire. The hero — Seran, Marcus had learned — was watching this exchange with the focused attention of someone who understood that the most important information in a situation isn't always the loudest.

"Can you teach it?" Aldric asked. His voice had gone careful in the way voices go careful when you want something badly enough that wanting it is embarrassing.

Marcus thought about this honestly. "I can tell you the principles. Whether you can apply them depends on things I don't know about you yet."

"What things?"

"Whether you think about magic as a force you invoke or a system you interact with." He looked at Aldric directly. "Those aren't the same orientation. Most of what I do won't make sense until the second one is natural."

Aldric opened his mouth. Closed it. Looked at his hands the way people look at tools they've been using incorrectly for twenty years.

"I've been studying magic for thirty-one years," he said. "I hold the highest certification my order grants."

"I know," Marcus said, which was not quite true — he'd inferred it from Aldric's mana structure, which was dense and deep and arranged with the particular architecture of someone who had learned from an institution rather than first principles. Very good within its own frame. The frame itself was the problem.

He tried to think of a kind way to say this.

"Your magic is excellent," he said. "Your model of what magic is is roughly where medicine was before people understood what caused disease. Effective treatments existed. The reasoning behind them was wrong."

Aldric was quiet for a long time.

"How wrong?" he finally said.

Marcus considered how to answer. Then he did what had always come most naturally to him, which was to show rather than explain.

He reached up and pulled the air apart in a small space above the fire, below where they could all see it, and he let it be visible — the actual substrate, the way mana was before it became anything, the real structure underneath the language humans and demons both used to describe what they were doing without understanding what they were doing.

He touched it. Just touched it, the way you'd rest a finger on a table. Nothing dramatic. Just contact.

Aldric made a sound Marcus had not heard from a human before. It was the kind of sound that didn't have a clear emotional category — somewhere between awe and vertigo and the particular grief of realizing that a thing you loved was much larger than you'd known.

"Oh," Aldric said.

"Yes," Marcus said.

"We don't— none of us— we've never—"

"I know."

He let the air close again and put the mana back the way it had been. The fire crackled normally. The smoke continued to avoid their faces.

Aldric sat with his hands in his lap and looked at the middle distance with the expression of a man quietly rebuilding something large.

The other two had been watching all of this. Yara, the archer, who fought with a sharp economy of motion that Marcus had clocked as self-taught rather than trained — she'd had to invent her own techniques because no one in her life had known enough to teach her. And then the fourth one: Deren, the healer, who had not spoken very much at all and who Marcus kept noticing in the way you notice a variable that isn't behaving the way the type signature implies.

Deren said, carefully: "Where are you from."

It was phrased as a question but it had the structure of something that had been worked up to.

"Another world," Marcus said. "Different one from this."

"You've mentioned that. I mean—" Deren paused. "What was it like."

Marcus looked at the fire.

He had not actually thought about this. He had been thinking forward so consistently and with such focus that backward had largely gone unexamined, which was typical of how he operated but perhaps not entirely healthy, he dimly recognized.

"Loud," he said. "Very loud. A lot of people. A lot of information, all the time." He turned his hand over, looking at the grey-black skin of Grak's fingers, which were his fingers now. "I didn't have many people I was close to there. I didn't really notice. I had work."

"Do you miss it?" Deren asked.

A genuine question. Not a trap, not a test. Just someone trying to understand what was sitting across the fire from them.

"I miss the infrastructure," Marcus said. "The accumulated knowledge. Libraries that held everything humans had ever figured out. Tools built on top of other tools going back centuries." He paused. "I don't miss being about to retire into doing nothing. That was — I don't think I actually wanted that. I think I wanted the problem of how to get there to be solved."

Seran said, quietly: "You were building toward an ending."

"I was optimizing toward a local maximum," Marcus said. "Which is a different thing."

Seran seemed to think about this. He had a good mind, Marcus had decided — not analytically inclined the way Aldric was, but broadly intelligent in the practical way of someone who had survived things by paying attention. "And now?"

"Now there's no local maximum in sight." He looked at the fire. Something that wasn't quite a smile moved across Grak's face, which was his face now. "That's better, I think."

He let them ask for another two hours. Aldric asked most of the questions, but the good ones kept coming from Deren and occasionally from Seran in the focused concise way Seran had of saying a lot with few words. Yara mostly listened with her eyes sharp and her assessment of everything ongoing.

Eventually, without quite deciding to, Marcus reached into the space above the fire and opened a window.

Not a spell. Not a construct. Just a projection — memory and mana structured into image, held in the air below where they could all see it.

A planet, seen from above. Blue and green and white.

"This is where I came from," he said.

They went quiet.

The planet turned slowly. He hadn't meant to animate it but the mana had a fidelity instinct by now, so it moved the way he remembered it moving, which was to say with the sublime slow indifference of something enormous and indifferent to being looked at.

He zoomed. Continent. Then country — he picked the coast, familiar geography. Then city.

The road structure came into view first. Then the density. Then the buildings, and he heard Yara inhale, and then the skyscrapers came into view and nobody said anything at all.

He let them look.

The city pulsed with its ten million lives. Lights and movement and the incomprehensible layered complexity of a civilization that had spent two hundred years industrializing and a hundred more after that building things on top of the industrialization. Bridges. Airports. Container ships in the harbor like grey islands with their own ecosystems of machinery.

Seran finally said: "Those towers."

"Buildings. People live and work in them."

"How many people."

"In this city, maybe ten million. The world held around eight billion when I left."

He watched them process the number. It didn't really land — he understood that. Eight billion was not a number human cognition handled gracefully. You'd need a different intuition for scale than most people developed, and even then it was more label than feeling.

"How did they—" Aldric started.

"Slowly. Over a very long time. And then all at once." Marcus looked at the image. "The magic there is different. It's not mana. It's physics — forces, materials, energy. They learned to read the rules of the physical world and exploit them systematically." He paused. "The results look similar from far enough out."

He closed the projection.

The campfire was very quiet for a moment.

Then Aldric said, in a voice that was carefully neutral in the way that means the speaker is not feeling neutral at all: "After seeing that. After telling us all of this. You understand what you're going to do to this world."

Marcus looked at him.

"By just existing," Aldric said. "By talking to us. By touching the mana where we can watch your hands." He gestured at himself, at the others, at the road behind them. "We're going to go back. We're going to tell people. And you're— what you know is going to—" He stopped. "This world is going to become unrecognizable."

"Yes," Marcus said.

"You knew that."

"I knew that."

"And you walked out of the castle anyway."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. The fire crackled. The feedback loops he'd built maintained their temperature differential with quiet efficiency.

"The alternative was staying in a castle full of demons waiting for something interesting to happen," he said. "That wasn't a serious option."

Seran looked at the road ahead, and then at Marcus, with the expression of a man recalculating a risk he thought he'd already assessed. "You've decided that changing the world is worth doing."

"I've decided it's probably going to happen regardless," Marcus said. "Given that, I'd rather be involved in how."

Nobody had a good answer to that. He hadn't expected one.
