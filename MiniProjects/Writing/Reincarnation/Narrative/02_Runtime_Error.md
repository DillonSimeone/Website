He heard about them before he saw them.

Castle politics moved at the speed of anxiety, which was fast. A summoned hero. A party of four. They'd already cut through the outer walls and were ascending. The Demon Lord was reportedly unconcerned, which meant the Demon Lord was concerned enough to perform unconcern in front of subordinates, which anyone who'd ever worked in an office would recognize immediately.

Marcus's job was to be on the fourth corridor. To fight the party if they got that far. To win if possible, slow them down if not, die if necessary.

He thought about this while he watched the other guards prepare. Weapons sharpened. Spells loaded. A lot of aggressive posturing that he recognized as the demonic equivalent of trying to feel less afraid.

He did not sharpen his weapon. He did not load a spell. He went to the fourth corridor, stood at his post, and thought about what he actually wanted to happen.

He did not want to kill anyone. This wasn't sentiment — it was pragmatics. Grak's merged memories had given him a rough map of the world's political situation, and the hero's party was, almost by definition, the kind of problem that metastasized when you killed it. You killed four people and suddenly there were eight. That was just how the narrative physics of these situations worked. He'd read enough fiction in his previous life to understand the archetype.

He also did not want to die. He had, against all expectation, a great deal of work left to do.

He heard them before he saw them — the particular sound of people who have been in sustained combat and are running on adrenaline and training. Then the door at the end of the corridor opened and four humans came through.

They were good. He could see that immediately. The swordsman at the front moved like someone who had been in enough fights that the fear had burned away and left something cleaner behind. The woman behind him had the focused stillness of a caster preparing. The other two were flanking in a pattern that said someone had thought carefully about how to navigate corridors.

They saw him. He saw them.

He reached into his pocket — metaphorically speaking; it was more like a fold in his mana architecture — and found the thing he'd built three months ago.

He held it up. It looked like almost nothing: a mote of pale light, the size of a coin.

He flicked it toward them.

He had not been entirely sure what it would do at human scale.

It turned out it would do quite a lot.

The mote hit the air between them and unfolded, which was the only word he had for it. Structure blooming outward from a point, reading the environment, adapting, filling the corridor with something that was not quite a cage and not quite a net and not quite a negotiation, but had elements of all three. It was reacting to them specifically — their mana signatures, their physical positions, the spells the caster had half-loaded — incorporating all of it, surrounding it.

The swordsman tried to cut through it. The thing flowed around the blade with something that felt disturbingly like attention.

In about four seconds, the party was contained.

Not harmed. Not crushed or burned or anything violent. Just... held, in a structure that had read their capabilities and built itself specifically to contain those capabilities. Like a type-safe container. Nothing getting in or out without explicit permission.

Marcus stared at what he'd made.

The party stared at him.

He became aware that he was, in the current situation, responsible for four human beings who were currently prisoners in a castle that would very much like to kill them.

Well, he thought. This is a problem I didn't scope.

He turned away from them and ran the variables.

Option one: kill them. Fast, clean, final. He thought about the cascade of eight becoming sixteen becoming a crusade and discarded this immediately.

Option two: hand them over to the Demon Lord. He thought about what would happen to them then and discarded this faster.

Option three: let them go where they were. They'd be found. Outcome identical to option two.

Option four: get them out.

He turned back to the party. They had not stopped staring at him.

"I'm going to put you to sleep," he said. His voice came out lower than he expected — Grak's register — but the words were his. "When you wake up, you'll be outside the castle. Don't come back."

The swordsman said something. It sounded like a question, or possibly a threat, or possibly both.

Marcus reached into the structure he'd built and adjusted a parameter.

They went to sleep.

He stood in the corridor for a moment with four unconscious heroes at his feet, in a demonic castle full of creatures who would happily kill all of them, and thought: I've made things significantly more complicated.

Then he started working on how to make things less complicated.

He carried them out one at a time in the deep hours, wrapped in a mana structure that made them invisible to casual observation. It took six hours. He put them in the field beyond the outer wall, arranged reasonably comfortably, with enough ambient warmth woven into the air around them that they wouldn't freeze before dawn.

He walked back to his post and resumed his duties.

Grak's background static said nothing, because Grak would not have known what to say.
