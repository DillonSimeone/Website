# First Data Point

The city came into view the next morning from the crest of a hill, and Marcus stopped walking.

The lenses grew from the corners of his eyes without him quite deciding to form them — a habit now, automatic as breathing, mana restructuring to extend his perception across distances and through walls and into the spectrum of information that normal vision left behind. Parallel views, each updating thirty times a second. Market district. Cathedral. Castle walls. A prison that he looked at for two seconds and then away from. Sewers. The particular energy signatures of magic being used by people who didn't know what they were doing with it, which was to say everyone.

The party had learned to notice when the lenses appeared. They'd stopped asking what he was seeing, which was sensible because the answer was always everything at once and it would take longer to explain than to just keep looking.

He looked for a while.

"Medieval," he said.

It came out flat. He hadn't meant it as a judgment — it was just a category, a data label, a point on a developmental curve he had a reference for — but he heard how it sounded in the air and understood that it hadn't landed as neutral.

Kings. Church from what he could see in the architecture and the way people moved around certain buildings — the deference, the geography of the town organized around the cathedral the way medieval European cities organized themselves. Superstition filling the gaps where systematic knowledge hadn't developed yet. A justice system that he'd glimpsed thirty seconds of in the prison and already needed to look away from.

Magic everywhere, used badly, understood wrongly, its actual nature invisible to everyone wielding it.

He thought about the projection he'd shown them last night. The city of ten million.

He thought about what it had taken to get there — not the miracles, the grinding accumulative work. Sanitation before medicine. Property rights before industrialization. Education systems before either. Centuries of incremental iteration on models that were slightly less wrong than the ones before them.

He thought about whether there was a faster path or whether the path was the thing and trying to skip it made something important fall out.

Is it worthwhile, Aldric had said last night, quietly, after the others had slept. Not quite a question. Building on this.

Marcus hadn't answered. He was still running the calculation.

Seran was standing next to him now, looking at the city with the eyes of someone who'd grown up in a place like it and knew exactly what it was. "You're disappointed," he said.

"I'm calibrating," Marcus said.

"Those aren't the same thing?"

Marcus looked at the lenses and the thirty simultaneous views and the cathedral and the prison and the badly-used magic and the market where three different people were selling three different fake cures for the same disease.

"No," he said, after a moment. "They're not the same thing."

He let the lenses dissolve.

The city sat in the valley below them, going about its morning, entirely unaware that something had just decided to pay attention to it.

That was about to change.

He started walking down the hill.

Marcus is patient. He's quietly building solution B in a pocket dimension as he watches the party trying to convince the terrified guards to let Marcus into the city along with the party.

He watches the citizens' reactions as the party is led down the road to the castle surrounded by many guards. A prisoner walk, instead of a heroic welcome, Marcus mutters to the party.

Marcus' calibrations are thrown out of arrays when the nobles and king in the castle panics, saying that the heroes have fallen in with the demons instead of just... Listening to the hero.

Marcus ignores the guards trying to arrest the party. He gazes at the party, as to say, what now?

The party notices that they're covered with barriers so dense that there's absolutely no chance of any humans breaking through. Marcus is almost bored watching the nobles yelling and the knights attacking the barrier.
