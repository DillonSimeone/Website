# Recursion

They came back.

Of course they did. He'd known they would. But he'd needed time to think, and time to think required them not being dead, and this was the trade he'd made.

He defeated them again. Differently — this time he barely used the spell-entity, just modified the air pressure around them in a very targeted way until they passed out. Three seconds. It was almost rude in how fast it was.

He put them outside again.

They came back a third time and he watched them from the battlements for a while before they reached the corridor. They were better now — they'd adapted their tactics, braced for what he'd done before, prepared countermeasures for the specific things that had caught them. He found this interesting. They were good learners.

He also noticed, for the first time, that he was not finding this difficult.

He sat with that observation.

He had been assuming — operating on the baseline assumption — that his situation was roughly equivalent to Grak's situation. That he was a mid-tier guard with better-than-average magic ability who had gotten lucky a couple of times. The merging of memories had been so seamless that he'd calibrated his sense of his own capabilities against Grak's self-image, which was as distorted as anyone's self-image and also the self-image of someone who had never thought analytically about anything in his life.

He started, for the first time, actually measuring.

It took him two days and it was a somewhat disorienting process.

When he was done, he sat with the results for a long time.

He wasn't a mid-tier guard with better magic. He was — and this number felt absurd even as he calculated it — approximately sixty to eighty times more powerful than the Demon Lord. He'd been optimizing for so long, compounding improvements on improvements, that he'd drifted somewhere past the upper bound of the scale he'd been trying to use to measure himself. Several of his internal organs had at some point ceased being organs in the conventional sense and were now more like mana-processing architecture that happened to also regulate his blood pressure.

He couldn't tell when exactly that had happened. He'd been busy.

He looked at his hands for a while.

Then the hero party arrived in his corridor for the third time, and he sighed, and went to go deal with them.

This time he didn't immediately incapacitate them.

He stood at his post and looked at them and they looked at him, and the swordsman — who had clearly spent some time thinking about this encounter — started to say something prepared.

"I'm not going to fight you," Marcus said.

A pause.

"Yet," he added, because it seemed important to be accurate.

The caster's spell was still half-loaded. He could see the geometry of it. An interesting structure, actually — she'd derived something novel for dealing with the mana-entity, a kind of counter-optimization. He filed it away. The magic here was good. Humans built different things than demons did, approached the same problems from different angles.

That was interesting.

"You've come here three times," he said. "You've failed three times. You're going to keep coming."

The swordsman — the hero, presumably — said something that translated roughly as that's how heroes work and Marcus thought: yes, exactly, that's precisely the problem I've been trying to think around.

"What do you actually want?" he asked. "Not the mission. Not the quest. You specifically. What's the desired end state?"

That stopped them.

He watched them work through it. The hero wanted to protect people — that was legible in everything about him, the way he positioned himself in front of the others even now. The caster wanted to understand things, he thought, watching her eyes move over his mana architecture with an expression he recognized as the look of someone who has encountered a problem that doesn't fit their model. The other two were more opaque, but they were here, which meant they'd chosen this.

They wanted the demon threat dealt with. They wanted to go home. They wanted, underneath everything, for the world to be a place where this kind of thing stopped happening.

He thought about the Demon Lord in his castle. The various generals. The armies. The entire apparatus of malevolent hierarchy that he'd been living inside for the past year and finding mostly embarrassing in its uncomplicated brutality.

He thought about the math.

He said: "Follow me. Trust me for the next ten minutes. I'm going to solve this."

They followed him out. He wasn't entirely sure why — probably the sheer novelty of a demon asking for their trust rather than trying to kill them, combined with the fact that three failed attempts to stop him had recalibrated their sense of what options were available.

They stood in the field outside the castle.

He looked at the castle. Thick walls, deep dungeons, hundreds of demons, the Demon Lord at the center of all of it. A problem he'd been living inside without quite recognizing it as his problem to solve.

He thought about the spell-entity in his pocket. He thought about the iterations he'd run on it since he'd first built it. He thought about what it could do now compared to what it had done in that corridor three months ago.

He thought: okay. Let's see if this actually works.

He snapped his fingers.

The castle did not explode.

It did not collapse or burn or shake. One moment it was there — solid, lit with torchlight, present — and then it was not there, and the field was just a field, with a small, perfect sphere floating at about chest height where the main gate had been. The sphere was maybe the size of a melon. It was covered in layered geometric structures that shifted slowly against each other, and it emitted no light, and it made no sound, and it was the most complicated thing Marcus had ever built.

The hero said something. His voice was strange.

"They're all inside," Marcus said. "Alive. Time isn't moving for them. The outer barriers are self-repairing and keyed to my mana signature — nothing gets in or out without me." He looked at the sphere for a moment. The structures were holding. The entity inside was maintaining. "I could have killed them. This is harder."

The caster was staring at the sphere with an expression that he was starting to think might be permanent for her when she was around him.

"Why?" the hero asked. And then, which was a better question: "What do you want?"

Marcus looked at him. He looked at all of them — four humans who had come back three times to fight something they couldn't beat, because that was what they'd decided they were for. He thought about Grak's memories, the demon's narrow slice of the world. He thought about how little he actually knew about humanity. He thought about magic systems he hadn't explored yet, problems he hadn't found yet, structures he hadn't built yet.

He thought about how much more interesting the world became the further out you zoomed.

"I want to see if your people are worth the attention," he said, which was honest. "I've been in a castle full of demons for a year. That's a very small sample size and most of it has been unimpressive. I'd like better data."

The swordsman's hand was still on his sword.

Marcus noticed this and found it reasonable.

"You're afraid of me," he said. This was not a question.

"Yes," said the hero, which was the most impressive thing he'd said yet, Marcus thought. Accurate threat assessment and the honesty to state it plainly. That was something.

"That's appropriate," Marcus said. "I would be too." He started walking toward the road. "Come on. I want to see a city."

He heard them exchanging glances behind him. He heard the particular silence of people making a decision they weren't sure about.

Then he heard footsteps.

He didn't smile, exactly. But something in the mana architecture around him settled into a slightly different configuration — something that, if it had been written in code, would have looked like a program finally reaching the interesting part of its runtime.

He walked, and they followed, and behind them a small perfect sphere sat in a field, holding an entire army frozen in a single instant, wrapped in a barrier that would outlast them all.

The world was very large.

He intended to look at all of it.

On the journey to the city...

The demon had many questions. So did the humans.

They spoke at lengths over magical campfires.

The caster says to the party... The caster has realized that he never understood magic. He didn't understand a thing. Compared to the demon, the caster might as well just be eating mud. He's deeply embarrassed by how he never paid attention to how precisely magic worked. From just watching the demon touching mana, he's learning more than he has learned in decades. 

The demon is quiet for a moment, clearly thinking... Then he waves his hand and a projection of his old world appears below the humans. He explains, this is where I came from.

The world gently zooms down to a city. The party is already stunned into silent long before the skyscrapers comes into view.

After hearing the demon's story, it is obvious to the party that he's going to destroy this world. The knowledge he eventually will unleash, either by just being there, or explaining, will change the world into something entirely unrecognizable. They feel embarrassed about where the humans in this world are currently at with their magic and technology.

The jounrey is resumed.

The city comes into view.

Lenses forms around the demon's eyes, from the side, the party is able to see that he's scrying many locations at the same time, each lenses displaying a different image 30 times a second...
