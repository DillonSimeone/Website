# Two Motes

He came back through the wall the same way he'd left.

The party looked at him. He looked at them. Something in his expression said that what he'd seen in the dungeons had gone where the castle had gone — filed, contained, not forgotten, not being processed in any visible way because visible processing was a luxury and there was work to do.

"They're in orbs," he said. "All of them. Time-frozen." He sat back in his chair. "The physical damage needs experts in physical restoration. The other kind needs experts in minds." He paused. "Neither of those is my domain. We'll find the right people."

Deren started to say something.

"They're not suffering," Marcus said. "Time isn't moving for them. There's nothing to address until someone who knows what they're doing is there to address it." He looked at Deren steadily. "That's the right order of operations."

Deren closed their mouth. Nodded once.

Senna was watching Marcus with the expression she'd been developing since the throne room — not pity, not sympathy exactly, something more like the recognition of someone who has learned to spot the shape of a weight being carried by its outline rather than its declaration.

She said nothing.

Marcus leaned back.

"What changed my world," he said, to the ceiling, "happened twice."

He wasn't looking at anything in the room. The lenses weren't up. He was just talking, which was unusual enough that the room went attentive in a specific way.

"The first time, someone figured out how to make machines do the physical work. Not better than a person — differently. Continuously. Without rest, without hunger, without variance once the process was set. A machine that made things could run while the people who built it slept. Then while their children slept. The output compounded." He paused. "Before that, the limit on what a civilization could produce was the number of human hours available. After that, the limit was something else entirely. Resources. Logistics. Imagination." He turned his hand over on the armrest. "The material constraint on human life had been scarcity for all of recorded history. Suddenly it wasn't. Not overnight — gradually, then all at once. The problems that replaced scarcity were new problems and they were bad in new ways. But they weren't that problem anymore."

The fire was burning in its precise recursive loops. Nobody spoke.

"The second change," Marcus said, "was faster. Information — everything anyone had ever figured out, every question ever asked and answered, every mistake documented, every solution found — became instantly accessible to anyone. The isolation problem I described. The person like Cael asking questions into silence." He looked at Cael briefly. "That ended. You could find the other people asking the same question. You could build on what they'd built. The compound rate of knowledge went—" He made a gesture that didn't quite translate. "Vertical, approximately."

"Two changes," Seran said. "Production and knowledge."

"Production and knowledge," Marcus agreed.

He reached into the space beside his chair.

He held up a mote.

The magic users in the room felt it before they saw it properly — mana moving toward the point in his hand with the particular pull of something being fed, something growing, a structure of significant complexity taking on mass and intention. Aldric sat forward. Senna's eyes sharpened. Deren's head tilted slightly.

Marcus held the mote up between two fingers, looked at it, and then did something small with his other hand.

The mote split.

Two points of light, each distinct, each pulling mana with its own specific appetite, each radiating a structure that felt completely different from the other. One had the dense recursive quality of the spell-entity from the corridor — self-modifying, environmental, something that would grow in directions not entirely predictable. The other was different. Ordered. Indexed. It felt, to Aldric, like standing next to a library that didn't have walls yet.

"Production," Marcus said, indicating the first. "And knowledge." The second. He held them both, one in each hand, looking at them with the expression of a craftsman checking work. "The first one knows how to make things. Given material and instruction, it manufactures. Scales with available mass and mana. The second one knows how to store and share information at a rate and fidelity that doesn't exist here yet." He paused. "Both are seeds. They need somewhere to grow."

He looked at the party.

"Where," he said, "do we want a city."

The map unrolled itself across the table without being asked to — or rather it was asked to, in the sense that Marcus had decided it should exist and the mana in the room had updated accordingly. It wasn't a flat rendering. It was topographical, shaded, with elevation and water and what appeared to be population density indicated in a system of markings that the party needed a moment to read.

"Don't think about resources," Marcus said. "Don't think about land access, existing claim, building materials. Matter is matter. Within a clump of dirt there is—" he paused, selecting the right simplified version, "—everything you'd need to build anything, if you know how to ask for it in the right order. I know how to ask."

Seran was studying the map with the expression of a strategist. "Defense."

"Not a concern."

"You can't guarantee—"

"I can," Marcus said. Not immodest. Just accurate. "Walls are theater. The defense of this city will be what I put into those two motes, and that will be sufficient. If the party wants conventional walls I'll build conventional walls. I don't recommend spending the thought on it."

Seran looked at him for a moment, doing the internal recalibration that was becoming a familiar sight. He'd fought Marcus three times and lost in seconds each time and he still occasionally needed to re-update.

"Then what do we think about," Mira said. She had the map angled toward her, reading it with quick practical eyes.

"Factions," Marcus said. "The political structure of this world — where power sits, where it's fragile, where it's been sitting long enough to believe it's permanent. Whatever we build will be read as a threat by some of them and an opportunity by others." He touched the map at several points without quite marking it. "How people reach it. Whether it's a place you come to or a place that reaches out. Whether access is—" he paused, "—a gate or an open field."

"You want it to be a field," Deren said.

"I want to not make that decision alone," Marcus said. "That's a values question, not a technical one."

Aldric said: "The church will—"

"The church will do what institutions do when they encounter something they can't absorb or destroy," Marcus said. "That's a later problem. I'm asking about the map."

He looked around at them. The map waited.

"I could build it here," he said, with the neutral tone he used for things that were technically true and practically inadvisable. "Directly above this city." He looked at the ceiling and therefore through the castle and the sky above it. "Structurally trivial. Politically—" He seemed to briefly consider the cascade. "It would generate a significant number of simultaneous problems. I find problems interesting but I try to not generate them for entertainment." A pause. "Usually."

Cael let out a short sound that was almost a laugh and immediately suppressed it.

"Not here," Seran said firmly.

"Not here," Marcus agreed. "I'm the problem solver. I'm not recommending it. I'm noting it's possible because—" He stopped. "Because I've found that when people understand the full option space they make better decisions about which part of it to use."

"Where do you think," Mira said, looking at him directly. "You've looked at this map, you've been scanning the world since before we got here, you have information none of us have. Where do you think."

Marcus looked at the map for a moment.

He touched a point. Coastal, but not on any existing major trade route — adjacent to them, close enough to connect, far enough to not be already claimed by any of the major powers visible in the population markings. Elevated slightly. Good water. And something else, something he'd noticed from the motes — a low background mana density in the surrounding region, which in his current understanding of this world's magical ecology meant it was either ignored or actively avoided, and he had a theory about why that he wanted to test.

"Here," he said. "Provisionally. The isolation isn't permanent — the whole point is that it won't be — but it gives us time to build without being managed. And there's something in the mana structure of the region I want to look at up close."

"What something," Aldric said.

"Something that might be relevant to the knowledge mote," Marcus said. "Or might be nothing. I don't have enough data yet." He looked at Aldric. "Which is the normal starting condition for anything worth doing."

He held up both motes again. They turned slowly in the air between his fingers, pulling gently at the ambient mana, patient and complex and full of something that wasn't quite intention but was adjacent to it.

"The city will take the shape of what we decide it's for," he said. "Not the buildings — those are trivial. The shape. What it's trying to do. Who it's trying to reach." He looked at each of them in turn. "You've been in the world I'm building on top of. I haven't. Not really. I've been in a demon castle for a year watching people be simple at each other." He paused. "What does someone like Cael actually need to not have to figure everything out alone. What does someone like Deren need to do their work at the scale the work actually requires. What does a child—" He stopped. Looked at Seran's daughter, still asleep. "What does she need the world to be, when she's old enough to ask what it is."

Seran looked at his daughter.

Mira said, quietly: "That's the question, isn't it."

"That's the question," Marcus said.

The two motes turned in the air. The map waited. Outside the barrier the castle was processing its new and uncomfortable terms, and outside the castle the city was becoming evening, and beyond the city the world was doing what worlds do — continuing, complicated, unaware of the conversation happening in the throne room of a minor kingdom on a coast that nobody particularly important had thought about in a hundred years.

Marcus looked at the spot on the map he'd touched.

"I haven't named it," he said. "I don't think I should be the one to name it."

He looked at them.

Nobody said anything immediately, which was the right response. This was not a question to answer quickly.

He was fine with that. He'd been patient before. He could be patient again.

He put one mote in each palm and closed his eyes and waited for the party to tell him what they were building toward, so he could start figuring out how to build it.

The party chooses a location.

They watch with wide eyes as space folds around the party. The far becomes close. The horizon becomes the floor under their feet.

And there, they're at the location. 

Is this teleportation? Marcus, for the first time, sucks in air through his teeth and says, Teleportation is killing yourself, then rebuilding yourself at a new location. He deeply dislike this. It's sound, but... What he did instead was to just...

He holds up a paper with marks on the left repressing the party. He folds the paper, the marks moves over to the other side. He unfolds the paper, the marks are now on the right side.

He says that it's slower than teleportation, but it's more energy efficient since matter isn't being destroyed then remade.

Meanwhile, the party notices that they're now standing in a modern city. 

Eventually, the party notices that there's a pipeline sucking in water from the ocean. They're stunned by the elegance of the recycler system when they see how matters is being filtered and harvested from water. 

Why did humans waste all of this time mining when all of this was just... Everywhere? 

Marcus quickly figures out the abnormality in the background.

The time-sealed orbs are placed into a hospital, with instruments for the eventual doctors to unseal the orbs when they are able. 

Marcus explains that he has no interest in becoming the mayor of this city. He'll be living on the edge of the city instead, at the bottom of the ocean. It's beautiful gazing up and seeing sunlight being filtered through the rippling surface of the waters, through the kelp forests.
