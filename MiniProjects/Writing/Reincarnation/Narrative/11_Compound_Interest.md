# Compound Interest

The first people arrived within a month.

Not summoned, not invited by any official mechanism — there was no official mechanism, the city had no announcements, no ambassadors, no delegation sent to the surrounding regions with pamphlets. But word moved the way word moves, which is faster than anyone intends and in directions nobody fully controls, and the word was strange enough that the people who came to investigate self-selected sharply.

You heard: a city appeared on the northern coast. No one built it. It's full of things nobody has names for.

Most people who heard this nodded and went back to their lives. Institutions heard it and began their slow machinery of threat assessment. A specific kind of person heard it, the kind who had been lying awake asking questions into silence, and was on the road within the week.

Deren received them.

This had not been planned. It had emerged from the first month of the city's existence, during which the party had been collectively figuring out what they were for in a place that mostly ran itself — the production mote handled manufacture, the knowledge mote was building its index, the infrastructure maintained without maintenance — and Deren had simply been the person standing near the entrance when the first arrivals came through, and had been good at it, and had kept doing it.

The receiving wasn't passive. Deren talked to everyone who arrived. Long conversations, detailed, structured around a set of questions that evolved over the first weeks as Deren learned what information was actually useful versus what seemed useful. Where they'd come from. What they'd been doing. What questions they'd been asking that no one could answer.

The last question produced everything.

A wool dyer from a coastal town who had been trying for fifteen years to understand why certain mineral combinations produced colors that shouldn't have been possible under his model of how dyes worked, and had filled twelve notebooks with observations nobody had ever asked to see.

A woman who had been a healer's assistant for twenty years and had kept meticulous records of which treatments worked and which didn't and had noticed patterns that contradicted her order's official doctrine and had told no one because the last person who'd contradicted official doctrine had lost their license to practice.

A former soldier with a missing hand who had taught himself to read late in life and had subsequently read everything available to him and arrived carrying a list of contradictions between the historical texts he'd found, needing someone to tell him whether the contradictions were errors or evidence.

A child, eleven years old, alone, who had walked for two weeks and when asked why had said: I heard there was a place where they answer questions.

Deren sent word to Marcus about the child.

Marcus sent back a brief reply: find out what questions.

The questions, it turned out, were about plants — specifically about why plants grew differently in different soils, which was a question with a long and complex and beautiful answer that the child had independently begun to construct from observation without knowing it was a question that had occupied serious minds for centuries in another world.

The child was installed in a room with a window and a view of the kelp forest and access to the knowledge mote's interface, which Aldric had been developing with Marcus's input into something that could be queried in natural language rather than formal structure.

She became, eight years later, the city's first original research contribution to botanical theory.

Her name was Pell.

Aldric and Senna rebuilt the magic curriculum from scratch.

This took two years and several significant arguments, not between them but within each of them individually, the arguments a person has when they're dismantling the architecture of their own expertise to rebuild it on better foundations. Senna was faster at this than Aldric, which surprised him until she explained: I've known something was wrong for twenty years. I just didn't have the vocabulary to say what.

The vocabulary came from Marcus, mostly indirectly — he sent up written materials from the ocean floor with a regularity that suggested he'd been preparing them for a long time, covering the foundational principles of how mana actually functioned at the substrate level, stripped of the institutional language that had accumulated over centuries on top of the actual mechanics. No Fourfold Roots, no invocations, no ceremonial framework. Just: here is what is happening. Here is why it works.

The arguments that produced the curriculum were about sequence — what you taught first, how you built the scaffold, which intuitions you wanted students to have before they encountered the formalisms. Senna wanted to start with observation. Aldric wanted to start with principles. They eventually landed on a spiral: observation, then the principle that explained the observation, then a wider observation that the principle couldn't explain, then a better principle.

The first cohort of students was twelve people ranging in age from fourteen to sixty.

The sixty-year-old had been a court mage for thirty years and arrived expecting to find his existing knowledge confirmed. He left the first month's curriculum shaken in a way he'd need several more months to fully process. He came back for the second month.

He became one of the better teachers they produced.

Cael did not become a mage.

This became a point of some confusion among people who'd watched Marcus spend hours teaching him things in the throne room, who expected him to end up somewhere in the magical education system. Instead Cael disappeared into the production facility for six months and emerged with a working theory of materials that had no precedent in the world's existing knowledge base — not because he'd invented it, but because Marcus had left certain texts in the knowledge mote's index and Cael had found them and had, critically, already had the intuitions to understand them because he'd been building those intuitions by hand for years over a metal stall.

He started teaching what he knew to anyone who wanted to learn.

Not in a formal setting. In the production facility, working, with people standing around watching and asking questions, which was how he'd always wanted to learn and so was how he taught. The formalization came later, when there were too many people for one room, and the formalization was done by his students rather than by him because Cael found administration viscerally unpleasant and Marcus had told him once, in the ocean house, that the people who wanted to organize things should organize them and the people who wanted to discover things should discover them and the error was forcing the same person to do both.

The error, Cael came to understand, was one the previous world had made continuously and at significant cost.

Seran spent the first year solving problems.

Someone had to. The city ran itself in the sense that Marcus had built the infrastructure to run without administration, but a city was not only infrastructure. A city was the people in it and the disputes between them and the questions of how things were shared and who decided when the rules needed to change.

Seran was not a politician. He thought of himself as a soldier, and he was right, but he was a specific kind of soldier — the kind who had spent years making decisions under uncertainty with incomplete information and caring a great deal about outcomes for people he was responsible for. This turned out to be most of the skill set required.

Mira did the other half.

She had a mind for systems, specifically for the places where systems failed — where the theory of how something should work diverged from how it actually ran, where the gap between intention and outcome was quietly producing harm that nobody was looking at directly. She found these gaps the way a tongue finds a sore tooth, persistently, without being asked to.

Between them they built something that was not quite a government and not quite a council — a framework of deliberation that tried to make its own assumptions visible, to build in the capacity for revision, to resist the tendency Marcus had described in the throne room: pattern recognition past its useful range, categories that became walls.

They made it visible in the architecture. The central meeting space had no permanent seat of authority — the room could be reconfigured, the focal point moved, the implication of hierarchy redistributed depending on what the meeting was for. This was Mira's idea. Seran had thought it was too subtle. Eighteen months later he admitted it had been right.

Yara's brother became very important in ways nobody had predicted.

Cael understood materials and production. He did not understand trade — not because he was incapable but because trade had never interested him the way the inside of a metal joint interested him. Yara understood threat assessment and the movement of people through space. Neither of them understood the specific problem of how you introduced a city that shouldn't exist into a world of existing economic and political relationships without triggering the response that ended new things before they found their footing.

Cael's brother turned out to understand this intuitively.

He'd run a market stall for twelve years. A market stall was, stripped to fundamentals, a daily exercise in reading what people needed, what they were afraid of, what they'd accept and what they'd refuse, and how to offer something new in the language of something familiar. He understood that the world outside the city was not going to become the city quickly, and that the gap between the two was not a problem to be solved but a relationship to be managed, and that managing it badly was how the city became a target.

He became, without the title being formally given, the person who handled the outside.

He did this with a patience and a warmth that Marcus had noted once, briefly, in the ocean house, as something he himself did not have and recognized was necessary. He traveled to the surrounding regions. He sat in taverns and at markets and in the antechambers of minor lords. He listened more than he talked. He brought things back — not goods, mostly, but information, specifically the information about what people were afraid of, because fear was the thing that made new things dangerous and you could not address fear you didn't understand.

His name was Cael, too, which caused confusion, so people started calling them by their last names, which caused its own confusion because neither of them had last names the way the city's growing population did, and this eventually resolved itself into the city's first informal naming convention, which was a small thing that somehow felt significant to everyone who watched it happen.

Marcus came up from the ocean floor approximately once a month.

Sometimes less. The party learned to read the difference between absence-that-was-fine and absence-that-meant-something-had-captured-his-attention-so-completely-that-the-external-world-had-temporarily-ceased-to-exist-as-a-relevant-variable. The second kind produced texts left at the shore for Aldric to collect — dense, not always intelligible on first reading, occasionally containing a breakthrough that restructured three months of curriculum work and required Aldric to sit down and not move for an hour.

When he did come up, he walked through the city the way he'd walked through the city that first night — not invisibly, not hidden, but with a quality of attention that made him easy to miss if you weren't looking for him. Watching things. Checking on the motes, which had grown far beyond their seed state and now ramified through the city's infrastructure in ways that would take a dedicated researcher years to map fully.

He stopped at the facility where Deren worked.

This had become a significant facility. The expertise the city needed for the orbs hadn't existed when Marcus placed them there, but the knowledge mote's index had contained the foundational texts, and Deren had spent two years building a practice around those foundations, and the three other practitioners they'd trained had built further, and the work had reached the point where the first orb had been carefully, slowly, successfully opened.

The person inside had spent three days not speaking. Then they had spoken, haltingly, to Deren, who had been there every day.

That was seven months ago. There were now two others who had been brought out. It was slow. It was supposed to be slow.

Marcus stood in the doorway of the facility and looked at what Deren had built and did not say anything for a while.

"The orbs are holding," Deren said, without looking up from what they were working on.

"I know. I can feel them." He looked at the room. The instruments, the records on the walls, the careful documentation of every step. "This is good work."

"It's slow."

"It's right," Marcus said, which was different.

Deren looked up. "The one we opened last month — she asked me a question yesterday. Asked why someone had saved her." They paused. "I didn't know what to say."

Marcus considered this. "What did you say?"

"That it wasn't about her specifically. That someone had decided it was more trouble than it was worth not to."

A pause.

"That's accurate," Marcus said.

"I know. I'm not sure it was what she needed to hear."

"What did she say?"

Deren was quiet for a moment. "She laughed. I think it was actually what she needed to hear." They looked at the wall of records. "She's been helping with the documentation. She has very precise handwriting."

Marcus looked at the documentation.

"Good," he said.

He left. Deren listened to his footsteps go quiet and then went back to work.

The city grew.

Not explosively — it had no announcement, no conquest, no authority compelling anyone toward it. It grew the way things grow when the conditions are right and the thing being grown is what the surrounding environment was quietly waiting to produce. People arrived. Some stayed. Some left and came back later. Some left and told people and those people came.

The word changed over time. First: a strange city on the coast, appeared from nowhere. Then: a city where they answer questions. Then, gradually, inaccurately but meaningfully: a city where you can go if you want to learn something.

The child Pell, now a young adult, was teaching three students of her own. Her research on soil composition and plant chemistry had been incorporated into the knowledge mote's index — she had been the first person to add original content rather than query existing content, which Marcus noted with what the party had learned to recognize as satisfaction, and which he expressed by sending up a text the following day that contained seventeen pages of related material she'd find useful.

She read it in one sitting and then immediately wrote back with four questions.

He answered all four at length.

Their correspondence, over the years, became one of the founding documents of what the city eventually called natural philosophy, which was the city's language for the thing Marcus's world had called science, the difference in naming reflecting a difference in relationship — not humanity against nature, extracting its secrets, but humanity in nature, noticing things and thinking about what they meant.

The king sent an envoy in the second year.

Seran received them. Mira sat in. The envoy had been given a mandate that was mostly about threat assessment — the city was too unusual to ignore and too useful to attack, and the surrounding powers had spent two years arguing about which concern was larger.

The envoy was a carefully chosen woman who had spent thirty years navigating exactly this kind of situation — neither hostile nor credulous, observant, with the specific patience of someone who has learned to let new things become intelligible before responding to them.

She spent three weeks in the city.

She sent back a report that Cael-the-trader obtained a copy of through methods he didn't specify and shared with Seran, who read it and then read it again.

The report said, among other things: I cannot adequately describe what I observed. The material infrastructure appears self-sustaining by means I do not understand. The population is small but growing and is composed primarily of individuals who appear to have been selected by their own inclinations rather than by external authority. What I can say with confidence is that this is not a military threat in any conventional sense. It is something else. I find myself unable to determine whether it is more or less concerning than a military threat, because I have no category for it yet.

She came back the following year on her own.

She stayed.

At the bottom of the ocean, Marcus read.

He read what the knowledge mote was collecting — not just the foundational texts he'd seeded it with, but the new things being generated by the city's growing population of people asking questions. He read Pell's soil papers and Aldric's revised curriculum and Deren's clinical documentation and a series of observations by a metalworker who'd been working with Cael for eighteen months and had begun to develop a theory about why certain alloys behaved the way they did under mana infusion.

He read Cael-the-trader's notes on the political situation in the surrounding regions, which were written in a plain style that concealed considerable sophistication, and which contained an observation in the most recent installment that made Marcus pause.

The fear is decreasing, Cael had written. Not gone. I don't think it goes away, exactly. But the shape of it is changing. Two years ago people were afraid of what the city was. Now some of them are afraid of what it means — which is different, because you can argue with a meaning.

Marcus read that twice.

The kelp moved in the current above him. The surface light rippled and shifted with the movement of the water. A fish investigated his window with the incurious investigation of something that had decided he was not food and therefore not particularly interesting.

He thought about Cael-the-trader, who had run a market stall and taught himself to read the room and was now reading an entire geopolitical situation and writing about it with the matter-of-fact clarity of someone who had found their actual scale.

He thought about what it took to find your actual scale — to discover that the thing you'd been doing in a small context was in fact a significant capability that a larger context could use, and to make that transition without losing the thing that had made the capability real in the first place.

He thought about how the city was already doing this, quietly, for more people than he was directly tracking.

He turned back to the metalworker's paper on mana-infused alloys.

The core observation was correct. The interpretation was a first draft. He wrote back three pages of notes — not corrections, redirections, here is a question to ask next, here is what the answer to that question will tell you, here is why that matters.

He had, he realized, been doing this for eighteen months without quite acknowledging it as a pattern. The notes going up from the ocean floor. The curriculum material. Pell's correspondence. The slow, continuous, low-bandwidth work of helping people build the scaffold underneath what they already knew.

Teaching, he thought, with the slight internal resistance of someone identifying a word that was correct but personally uncomfortable.

He was, apparently, teaching.

He thought about what Grak would have made of this and found that Grak was now so far background that he couldn't even construct the imagined reaction.

He started writing the next set of curriculum materials for Aldric.

Outside, the kelp forest moved in its slow enormous way. The light came down from the surface in long angled shafts, green and shifting and ancient, illuminating nothing in particular and everything in it.

The city hummed, forty meters above, and continued to become what it was going to become.

Marcus kept writing.

War.

Kingdoms have branded together.

Marcus is unbothered. He offers options when the party visits him under the ocean. The party is stunned by what has been happening on the ocean floor, they thought the city above was a miracle... What they're seeing down here is the true miracle. Marcus is oddly cheerful. 

The options he offers are as follows:

Let the army approach, and he can just seal them all away into orbs for the city to deal with one by one under its justice system.

Never let the army approach, the horizon always move away from them. They're eventually forced to return home.

Let the army approach, and just ignore them. They won't be able to break the barriers, or harm anyone coming to or going from the city.

Send a golem army to meet the army in field. The golems wouldn't kill or attack anyone... Instead, the golems would capture everyone one by one and just slowly walk them all back home before dumping them out onto the ground and leaving. The golems could even have tanks of water and a fridge filled with good foods in them, along with waste processing to ensure that the army is returned home in good health. Maybe fill them with books? It'd be easy to rise 90,000 golems almost overnight, a factory just have to be built, fed matter from the air and ocean then simply turned on and left running.

It is clear to everyone that Marcus likes the golem idea the most. Marcus is just not sure if it'd be too... Embarrassing for the kingdoms? If it will escalate things further. But it will be funny if this is the solution! It's exactly what he have always wanted to do in his previous world... Golems outnumbering the native population infinite to one. Back in his world, they called those robots. 

For the first time, Marcus slightly loses focus and start talking about a Howl's Moving castle, for a moment before refocusing on the subject.
