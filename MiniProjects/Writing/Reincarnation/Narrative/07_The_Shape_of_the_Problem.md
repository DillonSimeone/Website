# The Shape of the Problem

The chairs arrived the way everything Marcus made arrived — without drama, just suddenly present, as if they'd always been there and the room had only just remembered them. Good chairs. The kind with the right amount of give in the back. A low table between them with things on it that took the party a moment to identify as food — not the local variety, something else, compact and wrapped, from a different context entirely.

Books stacked beside Marcus's chair, already open to various pages, which implied he'd been reading them simultaneously with everything else.

He was looking at the ceiling.

The throne room beyond the barrier continued its silent theater. The king had sat down. The advisors were having what looked like an argument among themselves now, which was probably an improvement.

"When they calm down," Marcus said, "however long that takes. A few hours, probably. You'll have a chance to actually talk to them." He turned a page without looking at it. "If they want to talk. The wanting is not guaranteed."

"They'll want to talk," Seran said. "They'll need something from you at some point. That's when wanting happens."

"Usually," Marcus agreed.

He was quiet for a moment. Still looking at the ceiling. The lenses had retracted but there was something in his expression that said he was looking at more than plaster.

"This happened where I came from," he said. "Not once. Many times. Different places, different centuries, same structure." He turned another page. "Someone unfamiliar arrives. Institutions respond with containment. Containment fails or isn't appropriate. The institution needs to explain the failure. It needs something to blame that isn't itself."

He paused.

"The people who let the unfamiliar thing in," he said. "The people who vouched for it. The people who associated with it." He looked at Seran. "They become the explanation."

The room was quiet.

"I looked at the dungeons when we came through the gate," Marcus said. "I had the mote running. I was gathering data."

He stopped looking at the ceiling.

"I almost destroyed the castle," he said, in the same tone he'd used for everything else. Flat, factual, the tone of someone reporting a measurement. "I was—" He paused. Chose words with what felt like unusual care. "The demons I lived with for a year killed. They were brutal in the way of something with no particular imagination — they hit things until the things stopped. I found it embarrassing. Base." He looked at his hands. "What's in the dungeons below us is different. That requires imagination. That requires sustained attention. Someone has to care enough to continue."

Deren had gone very still.

"I can show you," Marcus said. "If you want to understand what I'm talking about. But if you're going to— eat something beforehand is not the right advice. Don't eat. Eat afterward. If you can."

Nobody asked to see. Nobody said they didn't want to see. The offer sat there on the table between them like a weight nobody wanted to pick up yet.

"I won't let that happen to you," Marcus said. He said it without inflection, which made it land harder than emphasis would have. "That's not— I'm stating it as a fact, not a reassurance. It will not happen. I want that to be clear before we discuss what comes next."

He straightened slightly in the chair. Something in the shift of his attention that the party had learned to read as him reorienting from internal processing to the present room.

"Do you have family here," he said. "Any of you. People you'd have gone to after this."

A beat.

Seran said: "My wife. She came with us — she's not a fighter, she helps with logistics, supplies. She'd have stayed at the inn when we went to—" He stopped. "She's in the city."

"Anyone else with her?"

"Our daughter. She's four."

Marcus looked at nothing for a moment.

"Yara," he said.

Yara's voice was careful. "Brother. He runs a stall in the market district. I saw it when we came through." A pause. "I saw it was still open."

"Deren."

"No one here." Deren said it simply. "I grew up south. There's no one."

"Aldric."

Aldric had been staring at the table. He looked up. "My order has a chapter house here. Twelve people. My teacher is— she's been there thirty years."

Marcus nodded slowly. He was watching the far wall again, through it, the mote out in the city updating thirty times a second.

"There's a messenger," he said. "Left through the east gate about eleven minutes ago. Riding fast." He watched something the rest of them couldn't see. "Same each time," he said, almost to himself. "The institution needs to explain the failure. The explanation requires evidence of corruption. Evidence requires finding the connections." He looked at Seran. "They'll have a description of your wife from the gate records. Inn registrations are kept."

Seran was already moving to stand. The barrier was still up. He sat back down with the expression of a man who had run into a wall he'd forgotten was there.

"I know," Marcus said. "Wait. I'll get them."

"All of them," Seran said. "My daughter—"

"All of them," Marcus said. There was nothing in his voice that sounded like it was a significant request. "Give me names. Give me locations if you know them."

They gave him names.

He sent the motes out through the stone like water through cracks, silent, invisible, finding each person by mana signature cross-referenced against descriptions, location data, the small specific gravity of people going about mornings that were about to become something else.

Seran's wife — Mira — was at the inn, which was close to the market, which was not yet in the radius of whatever had been dispatched. Seran's daughter was asleep. Marcus watched her for a moment longer than necessary. Four years old. A small even mana signature, the kind children had before anyone taught them anything about what it was.

Yara's brother was closing his stall because people were closing things, the general anxiety of the city becoming behavioral. He was fast about it, which said good instincts.

Aldric's teacher was at the chapter house, which was already being watched. Two men on the street outside not looking at it in the way that meant they were looking at it.

Marcus moved them all.

Not dramatically. No light, no sound, no sensation for anyone nearby. Just people suddenly elsewhere — the same technology as the castle in the field, applied at small scale, personal scale. Mira and the daughter arrived in the throne room still in the shape of what they'd been doing — Mira in motion, mid-step, a traveling bag already in her hand like some part of her had known. She startled violently and then saw Seran and crossed the room in four steps.

Yara's brother arrived confused and immediately looked at Yara with an expression that said he trusted her enough to be confused in her direction.

Aldric's teacher arrived sitting down, which was how she'd been, and looked around the room with the unhurried assessment of someone very old who has decided that panic is a young person's response. She looked at Marcus for a long time. He looked back. She nodded once, slightly, and then looked at Aldric with an expression that said you have a great deal to explain and I am prepared to listen.

The child was still asleep. He'd kept her asleep, gentle and complete. Mira held her against her shoulder without waking her.

Seran looked at Marcus over his daughter's head. "Thank you."

Marcus had already gone back to looking at the ceiling. "It's practical," he said.

"It's not only practical."

Marcus didn't answer this. He turned a page.

"You said," Deren started, and then stopped. Tried again. "In your world. You were — removing yourself. You said you were getting away."

"Trying to," Marcus said. "I'd almost made it financially viable."

"From what?"

He was quiet for a long moment.

"The same basic problem," he said. "Different surface. Institutions that process novelty as threat. People who've found a working pattern and protect it past the point where it's working." He looked at the wall. "I was good at what I did. That should have been straightforwardly fine. It was not always straightforwardly fine. Occasionally the fact of being competent at things people didn't understand was — an issue, for certain people. For certain institutions."

"So you decided to leave," Deren said.

"I decided to make it so I could leave," Marcus said. "Whether I actually would have—" He turned a page that he may or may not have read. "I think I would have found another problem. I always found another problem."

Aldric's teacher — her name was Senna, Aldric had said, and she had the mana structure of someone who had been working seriously for fifty years — was watching Marcus with the same focused attention she'd been giving everything since she arrived. Now she spoke for the first time.

"You've decided something," she said. "About this city."

Marcus looked at her.

"Not what to do about the men outside," she said. "Something larger. You were deciding when we came in and you're still deciding now and I think you've just about finished."

A pause.

"Aldric has told us about you," she said. "Not in words. In the way he's been sitting since he arrived in this room. I've known him thirty years." She folded her hands. "What have you decided?"

Marcus looked at the ceiling one more time.

Beyond the sound barrier, the king had stopped talking. The advisors had stopped arguing. The room had gone into the particular stillness of people who have run out of response and are waiting for the situation to tell them what to do next.

"That the problem isn't the king," Marcus said. "The king is a pressure valve. Remove him, get another one, same pressure. The problem is the shape of what people here believe is possible. What they think magic is. What they think knowledge is for. What they think—" He paused. "What they think change is."

He sat forward.

"Catastrophic update is one method," he said. "I do something visible enough that the categories break and have to be rebuilt. Fast, efficient, messy. High variance on outcomes. I've been—" He stopped. "I've been trying to decide if there's a slower way with better error bounds."

Senna looked at him steadily. "And?"

"The problem with the slow way," Marcus said, "is that the dungeons are still running while I'm being patient." He looked at the floor, through it, down into the stone. "That's a hard variable to hold constant while I optimize for something else."

Mira was looking at him. The child was still asleep against her shoulder. Yara's brother hadn't said anything since he'd arrived but he was watching Marcus with the same expression the apprentice on the road had had — that particular quality of someone deciding whether to be frightened or interested and landing, provisionally, on interested.

"What's your name?" Marcus asked him.

"Cael," he said.

"What do you sell at the stall."

"Tools," Cael said, slightly surprised. "Metalwork, mostly. Some I make myself."

Marcus looked at him for a moment.

"How much do you know about how the tools work?" he said. "Not how to use them. How they work."

Cael blinked. "I mean — I make them. I know how they're made."

"Do you ever think about why the process works? The heating, the shaping. What's happening in the metal when you do those things."

Cael looked at his sister. Yara looked back with an expression that said she genuinely didn't know where this was going.

"Yes," he said, slowly. "I think about it. I've never had anyone to—" He stopped. "There isn't really anyone to ask."

Marcus nodded once.

He reached out and picked up one of the books from the stack. He looked at it — not at the pages, at the whole thing, at what kind of object it was and what kind of world produced it and what kind of world it was possible to produce.

"There isn't yet," he said.

He put the book down.

"When the king decides to talk," he said to Seran, "I'll let the sound barrier down. Say what you have to say. I'll be here." He leaned back again, looking at the ceiling. "I expect it'll take a while. These conversations usually do."

Senna was still watching him. "And while we wait?"

"I have a question for you," Marcus said. "About your order's approach to healing constructs. I noticed something in Deren's mana structure when we came in — a base pattern I think came from formal training. I want to understand where it started before I—" He paused. "Before I suggest why it might be structured differently."

Senna looked at Deren. Looked back at Marcus. Something moved across her expression that was not quite a smile — something more like a person recognizing weather that is coming regardless and deciding to face it standing up.

"Ask," she said.

Outside the sound barrier, the king looked at the shimmering air where his throne room's authority had stopped working, and tried to decide what to do about a problem that had no entry in any category he'd ever been given.

Inside it, a demon who had once been a programmer from another world began, slowly and with great patience, to find out who in this one was already asking the right questions.
