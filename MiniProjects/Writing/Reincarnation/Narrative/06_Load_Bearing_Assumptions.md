# Load-Bearing Assumptions

The guards at the gate had been a twenty-minute problem.

Marcus had spent the twenty minutes doing other things.

The pocket dimension was a recent experiment — a fold in space that he'd been quietly expanding and furnishing over the past two days of walking, accessible through a point in the air next to his left hip that looked like nothing from the outside. He'd been running a slow build in there. Nothing urgent. Just something to do while Seran talked to the gate guards with the patient careful energy of a man defusing something that was mostly made of fear.

The guards' problem was straightforward: they had a category for demon, and the category said kill it, and Seran was standing in front of the demon and asking them to not do that, and they didn't have a good internal framework for updating on new information when the original category was this loud.

Marcus watched them through a mote he'd left drifting at eye level while he worked.

The sergeant in charge had a good face for this kind of thing — the face of someone whose sense of duty was doing a lot of heavy lifting over the thing underneath, which was that the demon at the city gate was the most frightening thing he'd seen in his career. He kept cycling through the same three expressions. Resolution. Doubt. Resolution again.

Marcus respected the commitment.

He made a small adjustment to the dimensional geometry in the pocket, resolved a structural issue with one of the load-bearing folds, and let Seran keep talking.

They let them in eventually.

Not because they were convinced. Because Seran was the hero — certified, summoned, on record — and the theological and political architecture around the summoned hero made it very difficult to formally deny him entry to anywhere. The sergeant had clearly understood this and disliked it and made his displeasure visible in every aspect of how he arranged the escort.

Twenty guards, heavily armed, forming up around the party the moment they were through the gate. More falling in from the sides as word moved ahead of them down the road. By the time they reached the main thoroughfare there were forty, and people were stopping to stare from doorways and windows and the edges of alleyways.

Marcus walked in the middle of the formation and watched.

He watched carefully. This was the first data he'd have on the civilian population — not the guards, not the nobles, those were predictable, but the people who lived here and made the city run and had no particular stake in institutional responses. A woman with two children pulled them back into a doorway. A man at a market stall just stared, openly, with an expression that took Marcus a moment to read correctly — not fear, or not only fear, something closer to genuine curiosity imperfectly filtered through alarm. A group of what looked like apprentices of some kind, young, pressed against a wall, one of them craning his neck to see past the guards with an expression that was almost hungry.

Interesting.

The party was feeling the weight of it. Yara's posture had shifted in the way it shifted when she was calculating exits. Deren was looking straight ahead with the disciplined calm of someone who'd decided to trust a plan and was executing the decision rather than reconsidering it.

"Prisoner walk," Marcus said, not loudly.

"We're not prisoners," Seran said.

"Look at the guards."

Seran looked at the guards.

"Prisoner walk," he said, quietly.

"Instead of a heroic welcome," Marcus said. "Which is what you'd have had three days ago."

Seran's jaw set slightly. "The situation is unusual."

"I know. I'm not criticizing." He looked at the castle gate coming up ahead. "I'm just noting that the optics of this are going to shape what we walk into, and what we walk into has already been shaped by whoever ran ahead to tell the king that the hero party was escorting something in from the road."

Seran said nothing, which meant he understood.

The throne room had too many people in it.

That was Marcus's first read. Someone had panicked and assembled everyone — nobles, advisors, castle guards, what looked like two separate factions of clergy who were maintaining a hostile distance from each other while united in their response to the thing currently standing on the floor between them. The king was a mid-sized man in his fifties with the posture of someone who had inherited authority rather than built it and spent his life hoping no one would notice.

He started talking before Seran could. Or before Marcus could. Before anyone could, really — he was clearly running on adrenaline and the particular fuel of having already decided what was happening and needing to state it before the decision could be questioned.

The heroes have been corrupted. Or captured. Or deceived. It was a demon. Couldn't they see it was a demon. The church had warned about— was this not exactly what the church had—

Seran tried to speak three times. He was talked over. The advisors were escalating in the way advisors escalate when they sense the king wants escalation — feeding energy back into the room instead of absorbing it, amplifying instead of damping.

Marcus watched this.

He watched Seran's expression change as he realized that the thing he was trying to do — which was simply to explain, to provide information, to update the model — was not achieving anything because the model wasn't in update mode. The model was in confirm mode. Everything incoming was being sorted into what it confirmed rather than what it added.

He became aware that there were knights at the party's flanks trying to physically separate them from him. Moving in slow and careful the way you move on something dangerous.

He became aware that the party was also aware of this and were looking at him with expressions ranging from Seran's controlled concern to Aldric's barely-suppressed fury to Yara's steady professional assessment.

He became aware that none of it was actually a problem.

He built the barriers without really thinking about it, the way you lock a door when you get home — automatic, ambient, not requiring attention. Dense layers, self-sealing, keyed to include the party and exclude everything else.

The knight who tried to grab Deren's arm hit the outer layer and stopped moving forward. He tried again. He looked confused. He tried a third time with more force and then stopped and stared at his own hand.

Three other knights had reached the same conclusion in parallel. One of them tried a sword. The sword did nothing except give Marcus something minor to observe — the exact force distribution, the material composition, the quality of the steel, all of it available from the interaction. Moderate quality. The swordsmith had done good work with what the metallurgy here supported.

The room noise increased. The king was pointing.

Marcus put up the sound barrier.

Silence.

The king's mouth was still moving. Two advisors were gesturing. A clergyman had raised something that was probably meant to function as an anti-demon ward, and Marcus examined the mana structure of it for a moment with genuine interest — it was not completely wrong, there was real knowledge in how it had been constructed, it just had no chance of functioning against him specifically because it was operating on a model of what demons were that was like bringing a tool designed for a specific fastener and finding a completely different fastener.

He looked at the ward for another second and stored what was useful. Then he looked at the party.

The party looked back at him.

He let the look say: so.

Yara broke first, which he hadn't expected. "You knew this was going to happen."

"I suspected something adjacent to this, yes."

Aldric was watching the silent theater of the nobles through the sound barrier with an expression of exhausted unsurprise. "You could have warned us."

"I wasn't certain. And I wanted first-order data on how your institutions behave under novel input." Marcus looked at the king, who had escalated to standing now and appeared to be delivering something he intended as a speech. "I have it."

"And?" Seran said.

Marcus was quiet for a moment. He was watching the room but he was also, underneath that, watching himself — calibrating, checking his own reasoning for motivated errors, trying to make sure that what he was about to say was conclusion and not rationalization.

"Pattern recognition is an asset," he said, "until the pattern changes and it becomes a liability. These people—" he gestured at the room, "—are operating on patterns that have been reliable for a long time. Demon at the gate means attack. Hero associating with demon means hero is compromised. New information processed through old category."

"That's what everyone does," Deren said.

"Yes." He looked at them. "The question isn't whether the pattern exists. It's whether there's enough— slack in the system. Enough capacity for update. Whether the people making decisions here have any practice questioning the frame, not just applying it." He watched the clergyman with the ward, who had graduated to pointing at Marcus and speaking with great intensity at anyone who would listen. "What I'm watching is whether the update is possible in principle, not whether it's happening right now."

"And?" Seran said again, the same word, different weight.

Marcus turned to look at him.

"The apprentices," he said.

"What?"

"On the road in. There was a group of them — young, probably studying something, magic or craft or scholarship. One of them was trying to see past the guards to get a better look at me." He looked at the sound barrier, the motionless scene of institutional response on the other side of it. "He wasn't afraid. He was curious. That's different from everyone else I've seen since the gate."

The party waited.

"One data point isn't a pattern," Marcus said. "But it's interesting." He turned away from the throne room and looked at the wall behind him, through it, into the city beyond the castle. "The problem with places like this isn't the kings. Kings are a symptom. The problem is whether there's anything underneath that has already started asking different questions. Whether the pressure for update is building somewhere in the system even if it's not at the top."

He paused.

"Sometimes it is," he said. "Sometimes that's exactly what's been happening and the top just hasn't noticed yet."

"You're deciding whether to help it," Deren said. Quietly. Not a question.

Marcus looked at the city through the wall. The market. The sewers. The cathedral. The prison. The one kid craning his neck to see something he didn't have a category for yet and deciding that was interesting rather than terrifying.

"I'm deciding," he said, "whether help is even the right frame."

The sound barrier held. The throne room remained frozen in its response. Outside, the city continued its morning, ten thousand people going about the specific work of living in the medieval period, working with incomplete tools and wrong models and superstitions filling the gaps.

And somewhere in there, probably, some number of people who lay awake at night thinking there had to be a better explanation than the one they'd been given.

Marcus kept looking.

"Pull up a seat," he said, to no one in particular, meaning all of them. "I need to think."

Chairs teleports into place. Along with books. And snacks as Marcus leans back into his chair and stare at the ceilings.

Marcus says that once the humans outside calm down, however how long that takes, the party will probably be able to finally start talking. If they even want to talk. 

Marcus goes on to explain how this have happened in the place he came from... How people was brutally tortured, then killed after being ruined in each way possible. And explains that he knows that this will happen to them if he just leave without them... That he saw what's going on in the dungeons. He almost destroyed the castle when he saw. Almost. Even the demons wasn't this cruel; they killed instead of torturing. 

He reassures the party that he won't let this to happen to them. He can show the party if they want to see, but if they do... Don't eat. Eat afterwards. If they can. 

He asks the party about their family. Do they have anyone they care about in this city? They're likely going to be rounded up soon, Marcus says while watching a messager leaving the castle. It's the same each time.
