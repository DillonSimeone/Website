The Conservation of Grace

The basement of the Shinra Manor did not smell of old paper or the damp rot of subterranean earth. It smelled of ozone, the sharp, metallic tang of ionized air, and a profound, mathematical silence.

When Professor Hojo stepped across the threshold, his boots clicking rhythmically against the stone, he did not find a madman. He did not find a monster nursing a crisis of lineage. He found a soldier who had simply ceased to move.

Sephiroth sat on the floor, his long silver hair pooling around him like spilled mercury. In front of him, arranged upon the cracked flagstones, was an array of colored glass.

"You did not return your transceiver, Sephiroth," Hojo said, his voice dry, scraping against the quiet of the room. "The executive committee assumed you had succumbed to a cellular rejection. Or a cognitive break. Given your... unique pedigree."

Sephiroth did not look up. He was staring at a flawless sphere of green Materia—a concentrated nodule of the Planet’s crystallized intent—which was suspended exactly four inches above the floor. It was not resting on anything.

"I found the records of the woman," Sephiroth said. His tone was devoid of the grandiosity Hojo had anticipated. It was the voice of a clerk correcting a ledger error. "Jenova. She was not an Ancient. She was an organism that fell from the sky, possessed of a singular, voracious directive: to consume the life of this world, convert its mass into kinetic thrust, and navigate the void to another. A celestial parasite."

"A beautiful, apex design," Hojo murmured, a thin smile twitching on his lips. "And you are her apex continuation. Did you not feel the call to inherit that legacy?"

"I did," Sephiroth replied softly. "I sat at the lip of the Mako reactor below Nibelheim, wondering what it would mean to turn this world into a vessel. I wondered where we would go. I wondered what a parasite does once it has consumed its host and find itself in the vacuum, waiting for the statistical improbability of another collision. It seemed... an remarkably inefficient use of mass."

He reached out a gloved hand, his fingers hovering millimeters away from the floating green sphere. Adjacent to it, a red Fire Materia and a blue Elemental Materia were wedged into the stone at precise, obtuse angles.

"To understand the vessel, I had to understand the fuel," Sephiroth continued. "Mako is the compression of consciousness into physical form. When condensed into Materia, it acts as a localized lens, forcing the ambient spirit energy of the world to conform to a specific thermodynamic law. A Bolt Materia does not create electricity; it creates a temporary gradient through which the Planet's potential energy can flow as current. It is standard work-energy equivalence. W=ΔE. The Planet loses an infinitesimal fraction of its vitality to produce a localized discharge."

Hojo frowned, stepping closer. The air grew warmer near the array. Not hot—just a steady, ambient hum of thermal pressure that shouldn't have been there.

"This is elementary, Sephiroth. You are wasting your cognitive faculties on solved engineering."

"It was solved," Sephiroth agreed. His green eyes, luminous and slit-pupiled, finally turned to meet the professor’s. "Until I attempted to map the lattice structure of a compound array. Look at the interaction between the paradigm shift of the support Materia and the localized gravity variance of the master sphere."

Hojo looked. He drew a small, brass-rimmed diagnostic spectrometer from his coat—an instrument calibrated to measure the exact mako-luminescence of the room. He pointed the aperture at the floating sphere.

The needle on the mechanical dial did not fluctuate. It pegged completely to the right, vibrating violently against the brass housing.

"The resonance is feedback," Sephiroth said, his voice carrying the terrifying weight of absolute certainty. "The blue Materia amplifies the output of the green. The green, in its altered state, shifts the gravitational constant around the red. The red emits a thermal variance that the blue registers as an increase in potential. I have checked the equations four thousand times in the dark, Professor. The input is a static value of twelve joules. The output..."

Sephiroth gently nudged a fourth, unaligned sphere of yellow Materia into the perimeter of the circle.

The air chimed. A soft, pale light bloomed from the center of the vacuum between the stones. A small, perfectly formed current of air began to circulate upward, spinning a stray piece of dust in a perpetual, unyielding orbit.

"The output is infinite," Sephiroth whispered.

Hojo stopped breathing. He dropped to his knees, his laboratory coat trailing in the dust, heedless of his dignity. He brought his face within inches of the floating glass. He checked the spectrometer. He checked the ambient temperature. He looked for wires, for a hidden battery, for the deceptive hand of a lesser magician.

There was nothing. The glass spheres were simply reflecting one another’s inherent geometry in a way that defied the very architecture of the universe.

"This is... a violation," Hojo breathed, his eyes wide, the pupils dilated with a mixture of horror and a sudden, manic ecstasy. "The First Law. Conservation of mass-energy. It cannot be bypassed. The Planet must be paying the toll somewhere. It must be draining the Lifestream elsewhere to compensate."

"It isn't," Sephiroth said. He stood up, towering over the crouched scientist, looking down at the tiny, spinning vortex of light. "I monitored the deep-core pressure valves in the reactor while setting the alignment. The Lifestream is unaffected. The energy is not being drawn from the world, Professor. It is being generated by the relationship between the pieces. The geometry itself is creating something out of nothing."

The great general of Shinra, the ultimate biological weapon, looked at his hands—hands built to sever, to conquer, to carry out the brutal will of an empire or the cosmic hunger of an alien mother.

"Jenova wanted to travel the stars," Sephiroth said, looking past Hojo, past the stone walls of the manor, toward the sky he had spent weeks ignoring. "She wanted to burn through worlds like coal in an engine, moving from one finite hearth to the next. Because she was a creature of biological instinct, and instinct understands only scarcity. It understands only consumption."

He gestured vaguely to the small, eternal light on the floor.

"But if the universe allows for this... if an arrangement of four stones can create a perpetual positive return... then scarcity is an illusion. The entire concept of a predator is a mistake born of incomplete mathematics."

Hojo was trembling now, his fingers twitching as he reached toward the array, desperate to disrupt it, desperate to prove that the universe was as cruel and transactional as he was. "We could power Midgar. We could power the continent. We could..."

"Midgar is an anthill, Professor," Sephiroth said calmly. His madness was not one of rage, but of an absolute, devastating perspective. "Shinra kills the Planet to sell its blood for profit. My mother wished to kill the Planet to travel to another. Both are the actions of primitives who do not realize the cage they are fighting in has an unlocked door."

Sephiroth walked past Hojo, toward the stairs that led up to the surface, leaving his legendary sword, the Masamune, propped carelessly against a stack of rotting books. He had no further use for an instrument designed to decrease the sum total of energy in the room.

"Where are you going?" Hojo called out, his voice cracking as he stayed transfixed by the impossible, beautiful light of the perpetual machine. "Sephiroth! What of your purpose? What of your birthright?"

Sephiroth paused at the foot of the stairs, looking up into the gray morning light filtering down from the manor above.

"I am going to find more glass," Sephiroth said. "I want to see what happens when I arrange them in a circle of twelve."
